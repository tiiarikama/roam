# Rocky Mountain National Park

## Overview

Rocky Mountain National Park protects 265,000 acres of the Colorado Rockies,
with over 60 peaks above 12,000 feet, alpine tundra, glacier-carved valleys,
pristine lakes, and dense forests. The park straddles the Continental Divide,
with the east side (accessed from Estes Park) featuring dramatic mountain
scenery and popular lake-dotted trails, and the west side (accessed from Grand
Lake) offering quieter meadows and river valleys.

Trail Ridge Road, the park's signature drive, climbs to 12,183 feet — the
highest continuous paved road in the United States — crossing above treeline
through vast alpine tundra. The Bear Lake area on the east side is the most
popular trailhead corridor, with access to a chain of alpine lakes, waterfalls,
and mountain views.

The park has over 350 miles of hiking trails, abundant wildlife (elk, moose,
bighorn sheep, marmots, pikas), and world-class scenery. Altitude is a defining
factor — most of the park sits above 8,000 feet, and many popular destinations
are above 10,000 feet. Visitors should acclimate gradually and be prepared for
the effects of high elevation.

## Activities

### Hiking

Rocky Mountain National Park is a hiker's paradise with trails ranging from
easy lakeside walks to strenuous peak summits above 14,000 feet. The park's
trails are organized into several geographic areas: Bear Lake Corridor (most
popular), Wild Basin, Longs Peak, Lumpy Ridge, and the west side (accessed from
Grand Lake).

Important safety rules:

- Altitude is the biggest challenge. Most trailheads start between 8,000-10,000 feet, and many trails climb above 12,000 feet. Acclimate gradually, drink plenty of water, and watch for symptoms of altitude sickness (headache, nausea, dizziness). If symptoms worsen, descend immediately.
- Lightning is the single most dangerous weather hazard. Afternoon thunderstorms are nearly daily above treeline from mid-June through August. Start hikes early (by 6 am) and plan to be below treeline by noon. If you hear thunder, descend immediately — do not wait.
- Weather changes rapidly at high elevation. Bring layers, rain gear, and warm clothing even on sunny mornings. Snow can fall any month of the year.
- Bear spray is recommended. While grizzlies are not present, black bears live in the park. Store food properly and keep a clean campsite.
- Stay on designated trails. Alpine tundra is extremely fragile — a single footstep can destroy plants that took decades to grow.

### Scenic Drives

Trail Ridge Road (US-34) is the park's marquee driving experience. Running 48
miles between Estes Park and Grand Lake, it crosses the Continental Divide at
Milner Pass (10,758 ft) and reaches a maximum elevation of 12,183 feet. Above
treeline, the road traverses vast alpine tundra with sweeping 360-degree
panoramic views. The Alpine Visitor Center at 11,796 feet is the highest visitor
center in the National Park Service. Trail Ridge Road is typically open late May
through mid-October, depending on snow — check the recorded road status line at
(970) 586-1222. Allow 3-4 hours for the full drive with stops.

Old Fall River Road is a one-way, unpaved historic road (uphill only, east to
west) climbing 11 miles from Horseshoe Park to the Alpine Visitor Center. It
provides a more intimate, uncrowded alternative to Trail Ridge Road. Open
seasonally (typically July through September). No trailers or vehicles over
25 feet.

Bear Lake Road is a paved 9-mile road from the Beaver Meadows Entrance to Bear
Lake. This is the busiest road in the park and provides access to the most
popular trailheads. During peak season (May 22 - October 18, 2026), a Timed
Entry + Bear Lake Road reservation is required from 5 am to 6 pm.

### Wildlife Watching

Rocky Mountain National Park has exceptional wildlife viewing:

- Elk: The park's most visible large mammal. Large herds graze in Moraine Park, Horseshoe Park, and Kawuneeche Valley. The fall elk rut (September-October) is one of the park's premier events — listen for bugling bulls at dawn and dusk. Keep at least 75 feet from elk.
- Moose: Most commonly seen on the west side in the Kawuneeche Valley and around streams and willows. Moose are unpredictable and can be aggressive — maintain a safe distance.
- Bighorn sheep: Seen near Sheep Lakes (spring), along Trail Ridge Road, and on rocky slopes. The Bighorn Crossing Zone near Horseshoe Park is a designated viewing area.
- Marmots and pikas: Found above treeline in rocky areas. Listen for the pika's distinctive high-pitched squeak.
- Black bears, mountain lions, and coyotes are present but less commonly seen.

### Fishing

Fishing is permitted in the park with a valid Colorado fishing license. The
park's lakes and streams hold brook, brown, rainbow, and greenback cutthroat
trout (greenback cutthroat are catch-and-release only). Popular fishing
destinations include the Big Thompson River, Sprague Lake, Dream Lake, and
streams in the Wild Basin area. Artificial flies and lures only in most waters.
Check the park's fishing regulations for specific rules by water body.

### Winter Activities

Rocky Mountain National Park is spectacular in winter. The east side remains
accessible year-round (Trail Ridge Road closes above Many Parks Curve). Popular
winter activities include snowshoeing, cross-country skiing, and winter hiking.
The Bear Lake area offers the most accessible winter trails. Rangers lead
snowshoe walks (equipment provided) on winter weekends.

Roads to Bear Lake and Moraine Park are plowed year-round but may require
chains or 4WD during storms. The west side is accessible from Grand Lake
year-round but Trail Ridge Road is closed. Backcountry skiing and snowshoeing
are available throughout the park with a wilderness permit.

## Overnight Stays

### Lodging

There is no lodging inside Rocky Mountain National Park. The gateway towns
offer extensive options:

- Estes Park (east side): The primary gateway town, with hundreds of hotels, cabins, vacation rentals, and restaurants. Walkable downtown. 5 minutes to the Beaver Meadows Entrance.
- Grand Lake (west side): A smaller, quieter mountain town on the park's western boundary. Hotels, cabins, and vacation rentals. Access to the Kawuneeche Valley and Trail Ridge Road.
- The YMCA of the Rockies (near Estes Park) and other lodges near the park offer family-friendly accommodations.

### Camping

The park has five campgrounds on the east side and one on the west side:

Reservable campgrounds (reserve at recreation.gov):

- Moraine Park: 244 sites. Open year-round (limited winter sites). The park's largest campground, located in a broad meadow with elk and mountain views. Near Bear Lake Road.
- Glacier Basin: 150 sites. Open late May through mid-September. Access to the free Bear Lake shuttle. Group sites available.
- Aspenglen: 54 sites. Open late May through late September. Near the Fall River Entrance. Quieter than Moraine Park.

First-come, first-served:

- Timber Creek: 98 sites on the west side near Grand Lake. Open year-round (limited winter sites). The only campground on the park's west side.
- Longs Peak: 26 tent-only sites near the Longs Peak trailhead. Open late June through mid-September. Popular with peak climbers starting early morning ascents.

Summer campground reservations open 6 months in advance on recreation.gov and
sell out quickly, especially for July and August weekends. Arrive early for
first-come sites.

### Overnight Hiking in Wilderness

A wilderness permit is required for all overnight backcountry camping. The park
has over 200 designated backcountry campsites and bivouac zones.

Permits are available up to a year in advance through the Wilderness Office.
Cost is $36 per party (2026). Popular zones (especially around Bear Lake
corridor destinations) fill quickly. Walk-up permits may be available for less
popular zones.

Bear-resistant food canisters or proper food hanging are required. Campfires
are prohibited in the backcountry (stoves only).

## Trails

### Bear Lake Corridor

#### Bear Lake Loop

- Distance: 0.6 miles (1 km) loop
- Elevation gain: 20 ft (6 m)
- Difficulty: Easy
- Time: 20-30 minutes
- Begin at: Bear Lake Trailhead (end of Bear Lake Road)
- Notes: The shortest and most popular trail in the park — a flat loop around a stunning alpine lake at 9,475 feet surrounded by Hallett Peak, Flattop Mountain, and Continental Divide peaks. Accessible (gravel surface). The parking lot fills by 8-9 am in summer; use the free Bear Lake shuttle from the Park & Ride lot. This trail serves as the starting point for many longer hikes. Even this short walk can leave visitors breathless at nearly 9,500 feet if not acclimated.

#### Nymph, Dream, and Emerald Lakes

- Distance: 3.6 miles (5.8 km) round trip to Emerald Lake
- Elevation gain: 605 ft (184 m)
- Difficulty: Moderate
- Time: 2-3 hours
- Begin at: Bear Lake Trailhead
- Notes: The quintessential RMNP hike, passing three alpine lakes in succession. Nymph Lake (0.5 miles) is a lily-pad-covered pool. Dream Lake (1.1 miles) sits beneath Hallett Peak — one of the most photographed spots in the park. Emerald Lake (1.8 miles) is ringed by dramatic cliffs and often still has ice into June. The trail gets progressively steeper and more narrow after Dream Lake. Extremely popular — start before 8 am or hike in late afternoon. The trail is icy in winter and early spring; microspikes helpful.

#### Alberta Falls

- Distance: 1.6 miles (2.6 km) round trip
- Elevation gain: 160 ft (49 m)
- Difficulty: Easy
- Time: 1 hour
- Begin at: Glacier Gorge Trailhead (or Bear Lake Trailhead, adding 0.4 miles)
- Notes: A popular, well-maintained trail to a picturesque 30-foot waterfall on Glacier Creek. One of the best short hikes for families and first-time visitors. The waterfall is most impressive in late spring and early summer during snowmelt. Can be extended to continue toward Mills Lake, Black Lake, or Sky Pond.

#### Sky Pond

- Distance: 9 miles (14.5 km) round trip
- Elevation gain: 1,740 ft (530 m)
- Difficulty: Strenuous
- Time: 5-7 hours
- Begin at: Glacier Gorge Trailhead
- Notes: One of the park's most rewarding and varied hikes, passing Alberta Falls, The Loch (a large alpine lake), Timberline Falls (a waterfall you scramble up beside), Lake of Glass, and finally Sky Pond, cradled in a dramatic cirque beneath the Cathedral Spires and Taylor Peak. The scramble near Timberline Falls involves steep, wet rock — use caution. Above treeline for the final stretch. Start early to beat thunderstorms and crowds.

#### Flattop Mountain and Continental Divide

- Distance: 8.8 miles (14.2 km) round trip
- Elevation gain: 2,849 ft (868 m)
- Difficulty: Strenuous
- Time: 5-7 hours
- Begin at: Bear Lake Trailhead
- Notes: A steady climb through forest and tundra to the broad summit of Flattop Mountain (12,324 ft) on the Continental Divide, with panoramic views including Longs Peak, Hallett Peak, and down to Grand Lake on the west side. Much of the hike is above treeline and exposed — lightning danger is significant. Start very early. From the summit, experienced hikers can scramble to nearby Hallett Peak (12,713 ft, adds ~1 mile round trip). The Dream Lake Overlook at mile 1.7 offers spectacular views.

### Wild Basin Area

#### Ouzel Falls

- Distance: 5.4 miles (8.7 km) round trip
- Elevation gain: 950 ft (290 m)
- Difficulty: Moderate
- Time: 3-4 hours
- Begin at: Wild Basin Trailhead (off Highway 7, south of the Beaver Meadows Entrance)
- Notes: A beautiful trail through forest along North Saint Vrain Creek, passing Copeland Falls (0.3 miles, easy) and Calypso Cascades (1.8 miles) before reaching the 40-foot Ouzel Falls. The falls are named after the American dipper (water ouzel), which can often be seen darting through the spray. Less crowded than Bear Lake corridor trails. Can be extended to Bluebird Lake (12.5 miles round trip) or Thunder Lake (13.6 miles round trip).

### Longs Peak Area

#### Chasm Lake

- Distance: 8.4 miles (13.5 km) round trip
- Elevation gain: 2,360 ft (720 m)
- Difficulty: Strenuous
- Time: 5-7 hours
- Begin at: Longs Peak Trailhead (off Highway 7)
- Notes: A spectacular hike to an alpine lake at 11,760 feet directly beneath the sheer east face of Longs Peak (14,259 ft) — the park's tallest mountain. The first 2 miles climb through forest; the trail then breaks above treeline into exposed tundra with increasingly dramatic views. A steep scramble over a rock field marks the final approach to the lake. Start before 6 am to avoid afternoon lightning. The Longs Peak trailhead parking lot fills very early (often by 3-4 am during peak climbing season for Longs Peak summiteers). This trail does not go to the summit of Longs Peak.

#### Longs Peak (Keyhole Route)

- Distance: 14.5 miles (23.3 km) round trip
- Elevation gain: 4,855 ft (1,480 m)
- Difficulty: Very Strenuous / Mountaineering
- Time: 10-15 hours
- Begin at: Longs Peak Trailhead (off Highway 7)
- Notes: The only 14er (14,259 ft) in the park and one of Colorado's most challenging peak climbs. The standard Keyhole Route involves exposed Class 3 scrambling with steep drop-offs, route-finding across ledges and gullies, and potentially icy conditions. This is not a simple hike — it is a serious mountaineering objective. The NPS recommends starting by 3 am to summit before noon and descend before afternoon storms. Helmets are recommended. The route crosses the Trough (a steep gully), the Narrows (an exposed ledge traverse), and the Homestretch (a steep slab to the summit). Conditions can change rapidly and dozens of people have died on this mountain. Do not attempt without proper preparation, fitness, and experience.

### Trail Ridge Road Area

#### Tombstone Ridge / Ute Trail

- Distance: 2 miles (3.2 km) round trip
- Elevation gain: 200 ft (60 m)
- Difficulty: Easy to Moderate (due to altitude)
- Time: 1-1.5 hours
- Begin at: Ute Trail Crossing pullout on Trail Ridge Road (above treeline)
- Notes: A short walk along the alpine tundra of Tombstone Ridge with 360-degree views of the Continental Divide, Longs Peak, and surrounding peaks. The trail follows the ancient Ute Trail across the tundra. Even this short walk feels strenuous at nearly 12,000 feet. Stay on the marked trail — the tundra is extremely fragile. Best in summer when wildflowers bloom. Exposed to weather with no shelter.

#### Alpine Ridge Trail

- Distance: 0.6 miles (1 km) round trip
- Elevation gain: 200 ft (60 m)
- Difficulty: Easy (but at 11,796 ft!)
- Time: 20-30 minutes
- Begin at: Alpine Visitor Center on Trail Ridge Road
- Notes: A staircase trail climbing from the highest visitor center in the NPS to a ridge overlook with views of the Never Summer Range and the Fall River cirque. Despite its short length, this hike is demanding at nearly 12,000 feet — expect to feel winded. A good stop while driving Trail Ridge Road.

## Permits & Reservations

### Timed Entry Reservation (2026)

A timed entry reservation is required from May 22 through mid-October 2026.
Two types of reservations are available on recreation.gov:

Timed Entry (standard):

- Required from 9 am to 2 pm daily.
- Provides access to all areas EXCEPT the Bear Lake Road Corridor.
- Best for: Trail Ridge Road, Alpine Visitor Center, Wild Basin, west side, and areas outside Bear Lake Road.

Timed Entry + Bear Lake Road:

- Required from 5 am to 6 pm daily.
- Provides access to the Bear Lake Road Corridor AND all other park areas.
- Best for: Bear Lake, Glacier Gorge, Sprague Lake, Moraine Park — the most popular trailheads.

Key details:

- Reservations are released in monthly batches beginning May 1 at 8 am MDT.
- Additional reservations released at 7 pm MDT the night before for the following day (sell out quickly).
- Cost: $2 recreation.gov processing fee (no additional NPS charge).
- Reservations allow entry within a 2-hour window. Once inside, stay as long as you like.
- No reservation needed before 5 am (Bear Lake Road) or before 9 am / after 2 pm (rest of park).
- Park entrance fee ($30 per vehicle) is separate from the timed entry reservation.

### Wilderness Permit

Required for all overnight backcountry camping.

- Available up to 1 year in advance through the Wilderness Office.
- Cost: $36 per party (2026).
- Popular zones fill quickly. Walk-up permits may be available for less popular areas.
- Bear-resistant food storage required. No campfires in the backcountry.

### Campground Reservations

- Reserve at recreation.gov up to 6 months in advance for Moraine Park, Glacier Basin, and Aspenglen.
- Timber Creek and Longs Peak are first-come, first-served.

## Seasons & Access

### Spring (April–June)

A transitional season. Lower elevations are snow-free by April, but trails
above 9,000 feet remain snow-covered into June. Trail Ridge Road typically
opens in late May (weather dependent — call (970) 586-1222 for status). The
Bear Lake area may have icy trails requiring microspikes through May.
Wildflowers begin blooming at lower elevations. Elk calving season. Fewer
crowds than summer but facilities are limited — some campgrounds and visitor
centers don't open until late May. No timed entry reservation is needed before
May 22.

### Summer (June–August)

Peak season. All facilities, trails, and Trail Ridge Road are open. Timed entry
reservations are required (May 22 through mid-October). Wildflowers peak in
late June and July in the subalpine and alpine zones. Daytime temperatures at
lower elevations are pleasant (70-80°F); above treeline expect 40-60°F with
strong wind. Afternoon thunderstorms are nearly daily from mid-June through
August — start hikes early and be off exposed ridges by noon. Bear Lake
corridor trails are very crowded; use the free shuttle and start early. The
parking lot at Bear Lake fills by 8-9 am.

### Fall (September–October)

The best time to visit for many. Aspen groves turn brilliant gold, especially
along Bear Lake Road and on the west side. The elk rut brings dramatic bugling
and herd activity (September into October) — Moraine Park and Horseshoe Park
are prime viewing spots. Crowds thin after Labor Day. Temperatures cool (50-65°F
days, 20-35°F nights). Trail Ridge Road typically closes in mid-October. First
significant snowfalls can arrive in September at higher elevations. Timed entry
reservations remain in effect through mid-October.

### Winter (November–March)

Trail Ridge Road is closed (west of Many Parks Curve on the east side, east of
the Colorado River trailhead on the west side). The east side remains
accessible via the Beaver Meadows and Fall River Entrances, with Bear Lake Road
plowed year-round (chains or 4WD may be needed). Snowshoeing and cross-country
skiing are excellent — Bear Lake, Sprague Lake, and Emerald Lake trails are
popular winter routes. Ranger-led snowshoe walks on weekends. No timed entry
reservation is needed November through late May. Estes Park and Grand Lake
remain open with reduced services. Winter backcountry camping requires a
wilderness permit and careful preparation for extreme cold and avalanche risk.

## Getting There & Around

### Getting There

- Nearest airports: Denver International (DEN, 80 miles / 1.5-2 hours), Loveland/Fort Collins (FNL, 65 miles)
- From Denver: ~1.5-2 hours via I-25 North to US-36 West through Boulder and Lyons to Estes Park (east side). Summer weekends can push this to 2.5 hours.
- From Grand Lake (west side): Enter via US-34. Grand Lake is about 2 hours from Denver via I-70 West and US-40 North.
- Three main entrances: Beaver Meadows (east, most popular), Fall River (east, less crowded), Grand Lake (west).

### Getting Around Inside the Park

Free shuttle buses run during peak summer season in the Bear Lake corridor:

- Hiker Shuttle: Runs from the Park & Ride lot on Bear Lake Road to Bear Lake, with stops at Glacier Gorge and other trailheads. Frequency every 10-30 minutes. Operates late May through mid-October. Using the shuttle avoids the near-impossible task of finding parking at Bear Lake during peak hours.

No shuttle service is available on Trail Ridge Road, Wild Basin, or the west side. A private vehicle is needed for these areas.

The Grand Loop (Trail Ridge Road from Estes Park to Grand Lake and back) is about 100 miles and takes 4-5 hours without stops. Plan at least a full day for this drive with stops at viewpoints and short walks.

## Practical Tips

- Timed entry reservations are required May 22 through mid-October 2026. Book on recreation.gov well in advance. Arrive before 5 am (Bear Lake Road) or before 9 am (rest of park) to enter without a reservation.
- Altitude is the defining challenge. Most visitors come from lower elevations and feel the effects above 8,000 feet. Spend at least a day in Estes Park (7,500 ft) before tackling high-elevation hikes. Drink extra water, eat well, and rest if you feel symptoms of altitude sickness.
- Lightning safety is critical. Get below treeline by noon during summer. If caught above treeline, avoid ridgelines, isolated trees, and water. Crouch low if no shelter is available.
- The park entrance fee is $30 per vehicle (7-day pass). Annual and interagency passes are accepted.
- Cell service is limited. Estes Park has coverage, but most of the park interior (especially Bear Lake area, Wild Basin, and Trail Ridge Road) has little to none. Download offline maps.
- There is no gas, food, or lodging inside the park (except the Trail Ridge Store near the Alpine Visitor Center, seasonal). Fill up in Estes Park or Grand Lake.
- The Bear Lake corridor is by far the busiest area. Use the free shuttle, start early, or visit in late afternoon to avoid the worst congestion.
- Pets are allowed in campgrounds, parking areas, and along roadsides but are NOT allowed on any trail or in the backcountry. Plan accordingly.
- Elk are common along roads and in meadows. They are large, wild animals — maintain at least 75 feet of distance. Bull elk are especially dangerous during the fall rut.
- Snow and ice can be present on trails at higher elevations well into June. Microspikes and trekking poles are recommended for spring hiking above 9,000 feet.
- Trail Ridge Road is spectacular but weather-dependent. Check the recorded status line at (970) 586-1222 before driving, especially in late May and October when closures are common.
