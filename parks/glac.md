# Glacier National Park

## Overview
Glacier National Park protects over one million acres of the northern Rocky
Mountains in northwest Montana, encompassing rugged peaks, pristine alpine
lakes, ancient glaciers, and dense forests. The park straddles the Continental
Divide and is often called the "Crown of the Continent" for its spectacular
mountain scenery.

The park is defined by Going-to-the-Sun Road, an engineering marvel that
crosses the Continental Divide at Logan Pass (6,646 ft) and is widely
considered one of the most beautiful drives in North America. The park has
over 700 miles of trails, 25 named glaciers (rapidly shrinking due to climate
change), and an extraordinary diversity of wildlife including grizzly bears,
mountain goats, bighorn sheep, and wolves.

Major areas include:
- West Glacier / Lake McDonald — the park's west side gateway, with old-growth cedar-hemlock forests and the largest lake in the park
- Logan Pass — the Continental Divide crossing, hub for the park's most iconic hikes
- St. Mary / East Side — the east entrance with dramatic prairie-meets-mountain scenery
- Many Glacier — often called the park's "hiking capital," with glacier-carved valleys, turquoise lakes, and some of the best trails
- Two Medicine — a quieter area in the southeast with lakes, waterfalls, and mountain scenery
- North Fork — the remote northwest corner with Bowman and Kintla Lakes

For 2026, the vehicle reservation system for Going-to-the-Sun Road has been
suspended. Visitors no longer need a timed-entry permit to drive the road, but
should expect temporary closures when parking areas (especially Logan Pass)
reach capacity. Arrive early for the best experience.

## Activities

### Hiking
Glacier has over 700 miles of trails, from short nature walks through old-growth
forest to all-day alpine traverses along the Continental Divide. The park's
well-built trails — many originally constructed for pack stock — feature moderate
grades that make long-distance hiking accessible. Most alpine trails are
snow-free from late June through October.

Important safety rules:
- All of Glacier is grizzly bear country. Carry bear spray, know how to use it, hike in groups, and make noise. Stay at least 100 yards from bears and wolves, 25 yards from all other wildlife.
- Weather changes rapidly in the mountains. Bring layers, rain gear, and warm clothing even on sunny summer days. Afternoon thunderstorms are common.
- Snow lingers on high passes well into July. Check trail conditions at visitor centers before heading out. Microspikes can be useful on steep snow crossings even in summer.
- Stream crossings can be dangerous during spring snowmelt. Some trails may be impassable until midsummer.
- Mountain goats are habituated to humans in some areas. Do not approach or feed them. They are attracted to salt in sweat and urine — urinate at least 200 feet from trails.
- Trail conditions change annually. Burned areas from recent fires may have downed trees and altered trail conditions. Check the Backcountry Situation Report.

### Scenic Drives

Going-to-the-Sun Road is the park's signature experience — a 50-mile road
crossing the Continental Divide at Logan Pass. The road climbs from the shores
of Lake McDonald through dense forest, past waterfalls, along sheer cliff
faces, and above treeline to the alpine meadows of Logan Pass before descending
to St. Mary Lake on the east side. Allow 2-3 hours without stops, or a full day
with hikes and viewpoints. The road is typically fully open late June through
mid-October (weather dependent). Vehicle size restrictions apply between
Avalanche Creek and Sun Point: no vehicles or combinations over 21 feet long or
8 feet wide.

For 2026, no vehicle reservation is required to drive Going-to-the-Sun Road.
However, a 3-hour parking limit will be enforced at Logan Pass starting July 1,
2026. Logan Pass parking (roughly 175 spaces) fills by 7-8 am on peak summer
days. Use the free park shuttle or arrive very early.

Other scenic drives include Many Glacier Road (12 miles from Babb to Many
Glacier Hotel), Two Medicine Road (7 miles to Two Medicine Lake), and the
remote North Fork Road to Bowman and Kintla Lakes (unpaved, high-clearance
recommended).

### Boat Tours
Glacier Park Boat Company operates scenic boat tours on several of the park's
lakes: Lake McDonald, St. Mary Lake, Two Medicine Lake, Many Glacier
(Swiftcurrent and Josephine Lakes). The boat tours on Swiftcurrent and
Josephine Lakes can be combined with the Grinnell Glacier hike to shorten the
walking distance. Tours run from approximately late June through mid-September.
Reservations recommended.

### Red Bus Tours
Glacier's iconic red "jammer" buses — vintage 1930s touring vehicles with
roll-back canvas tops — offer guided tours along Going-to-the-Sun Road and
other park roads. Multiple tour options from 2 to 8 hours. Operated by Glacier
National Park Lodges (Xanterra). Book at glaciernationalparklodges.com well in
advance for summer dates.

### Fishing
Fishing is permitted in the park with a valid Montana state fishing license
(no additional park permit required). Most park waters contain cutthroat trout,
bull trout (catch-and-release only), lake trout, and whitefish. No live bait or
lead tackle is permitted. Regulations vary by water body — check the park's
fishing regulations before heading out.

### Wildlife Watching
Glacier is one of the best parks for wildlife viewing in the lower 48. Key
species include grizzly bears, black bears, mountain goats, bighorn sheep,
moose, elk, wolves, wolverines, and mountain lions. The Many Glacier valley
is one of the best places to spot grizzlies, especially in late summer when
bears feed on huckleberries. Mountain goats are commonly seen near Logan Pass
and along the Highline Trail. Dawn and dusk offer the best viewing
opportunities. Always maintain safe distances and carry bear spray.

### Winter Activities
Most park roads close in late October or November. Going-to-the-Sun Road closes
beyond Lake McDonald Lodge (west) and the St. Mary entrance (east). The Apgar
area and the road to Lake McDonald Lodge remain plowed and accessible.
Cross-country skiing and snowshoeing are popular on unplowed roads and trails.
Backcountry skiing is available but requires avalanche awareness and equipment.
The park does not have a downhill ski area. Winter permits are required for
overnight backcountry trips.

## Overnight Stays

### Lodging
Glacier has several historic lodges operated by Glacier National Park Lodges
(Xanterra) and Pursuit:

- Lake McDonald Lodge: Historic Swiss chalet-style lodge on Lake McDonald's shore. Open late May through mid-September.
- Many Glacier Hotel: Grand lakeside hotel in the Many Glacier valley — prime hiking access. Open mid-June through mid-September.
- Glacier Park Lodge: Large historic lodge in East Glacier Park Village, just outside the park. Open early June through late September.
- Rising Sun Motor Inn: Motel-style rooms on St. Mary Lake. Open mid-June through mid-September.
- Village Inn at Apgar: Motel on the shore of Lake McDonald near the west entrance. Open late May through late September.
- Swiftcurrent Motor Inn & Cabins: Budget-friendly cabins and motel rooms in Many Glacier. Open mid-June through mid-September.
- Granite Park Chalet: Backcountry chalet accessible only by trail (7.6 miles via the Highline Trail). Bunks and meals. Open early July through early September. Book well in advance.
- Sperry Chalet: Backcountry chalet accessible only by trail (6.2 miles from Lake McDonald Lodge). Bunks and meals. Open mid-July through early September. Book well in advance.

Reservations open approximately 13 months in advance and sell out quickly for
peak summer (July-August). Gateway towns with additional lodging include West
Glacier, East Glacier Park Village, St. Mary, Whitefish, Columbia Falls, and
Kalispell.

### Camping
Glacier has 13 campgrounds with over 1,000 sites. A mix of reservable and
first-come, first-served.

Reservable campgrounds (reserve at recreation.gov):
- Apgar: 194 sites near the west entrance. Open early May through mid-October.
- Fish Creek: 178 sites on Lake McDonald. Open early June through early September.
- Many Glacier: 110 sites in the Many Glacier valley. Open late May through mid-September.
- St. Mary: 148 sites near the east entrance. Open late May through mid-September.
- Two Medicine: 100 sites. Open late May through mid-September.

First-come, first-served campgrounds include Sprague Creek (25 sites on Lake
McDonald), Rising Sun (84 sites), Avalanche (87 sites, near Trail of the
Cedars), Kintla Lake (13 sites), Bowman Lake (48 sites), Cut Bank (14 sites),
and others.

No hookups at any campground. Most have potable water and flush or vault
toilets. Reservations open 6 months in advance for reservable campgrounds.
First-come sites fill early, often by morning — arrive the night before or
very early.

### Overnight Hiking in Wilderness
A wilderness permit is required for all overnight backcountry trips year-round.

Summer season (June 15 - September 30):
- 70% of campsites available by advance reservation on recreation.gov. Lottery applications accepted in mid-March (check exact dates at nps.gov/glac).
- 30% of campsites held for walk-up permits, available on a first-come, first-served basis starting at backcountry permit offices.
- Cost: $10 per permit (non-refundable).
- Maximum group size: 8 people for advance reservations; 12 for walk-ups (varies by campground).
- Permits must be picked up in person by 4:30 pm the day before or on the trip start date.

Food storage: All food and scented items must be hung on provided bear poles
or stored in provided bear boxes at designated campsites. Bear canisters are
not required at summer-status campsites but are required at winter-status
campsites.

## Trails

### Logan Pass / Going-to-the-Sun Road

#### Highline Trail to Granite Park Chalet
- Distance: 15.2 miles (24.5 km) one way to Many Glacier (via Swiftcurrent Pass); or 7.6 miles (12.2 km) one way to Granite Park Chalet
- Elevation gain: 800 ft (244 m) to Chalet; significant descent beyond
- Difficulty: Moderate to Strenuous (due to distance)
- Time: 4-5 hours to Chalet; 7-9 hours through to Many Glacier
- Begin at: Logan Pass (Continental Divide sign, across the road from the visitor center)
- Notes: Widely considered one of the greatest hikes in North America. The trail traverses high along the Garden Wall — a knife-edge ridge of the Continental Divide — with dramatic cliff-edge sections, vast alpine meadows, and views of glacier-carved valleys in every direction. The first section includes a narrow, exposed ledge with a cable handhold (not for those with fear of heights). Mountain goats are frequently seen. An optional 1.8-mile spur from Granite Park Chalet climbs to the Grinnell Glacier Overlook for stunning views of the glacier and turquoise lake below. Most hikers take the free shuttle between trailheads for point-to-point logistics. Start early — Logan Pass parking fills quickly.

#### Hidden Lake Overlook
- Distance: 2.7 miles (4.3 km) round trip to overlook; 5.4 miles (8.7 km) to lakeshore
- Elevation gain: 540 ft (165 m) to overlook; additional 780 ft descent to lake
- Difficulty: Moderate (to overlook); Strenuous (to lakeshore, steep return)
- Time: 1.5-2 hours to overlook; 4-5 hours to lakeshore and back
- Begin at: Logan Pass Visitor Center
- Notes: The most popular hike from Logan Pass. A boardwalk-then-trail climb through alpine wildflower meadows to an overlook with views of Hidden Lake and the Bearhat Mountain spire. Mountain goats and bighorn sheep are commonly seen. Snow can cover the trail into July — be prepared for steep snow crossings. The descent to the lakeshore adds significant distance and a steep return climb. The overlook alone is worthwhile and manageable for most hikers.

#### Trail of the Cedars & Avalanche Lake
- Distance: 0.7 miles (1.1 km) loop for Trail of the Cedars; 5.8 miles (9.3 km) round trip to Avalanche Lake
- Elevation gain: Minimal for Trail of the Cedars; 730 ft (223 m) to Avalanche Lake
- Difficulty: Easy (Trail of the Cedars); Moderate (Avalanche Lake)
- Time: 30 minutes for Trail of the Cedars; 3-4 hours for Avalanche Lake
- Begin at: Avalanche Creek trailhead on Going-to-the-Sun Road
- Notes: Two great hikes from one trailhead. The Trail of the Cedars is a flat, wheelchair-accessible boardwalk loop through ancient western red cedar and hemlock forest — atmospheric and easy. Avalanche Lake continues uphill through forest to a stunning glacier-fed lake ringed by cliffs with multiple waterfalls cascading down. One of the best moderate hikes in the park. Very popular — arrive early for parking.

#### St. Mary and Virginia Falls
- Distance: 3.6 miles (5.8 km) round trip
- Elevation gain: 260 ft (79 m)
- Difficulty: Easy to Moderate
- Time: 2-3 hours
- Begin at: St. Mary Falls trailhead on Going-to-the-Sun Road (or Sunrift Gorge)
- Notes: A popular trail to two beautiful waterfalls. St. Mary Falls (1.8 miles) cascades into a turquoise pool. Virginia Falls (another 0.7 miles) is a tall, powerful cascade. Both are photogenic and the trail is well-maintained. Can be combined with a visit to Sunrift Gorge (a short walk from the road) for a third scenic stop.

### Many Glacier

#### Grinnell Glacier Trail
- Distance: 10.6 miles (17.1 km) round trip (from trailhead); shorter with boat shuttle
- Elevation gain: 1,600 ft (488 m)
- Difficulty: Strenuous
- Time: 6-8 hours (from trailhead); 4-6 hours with boat
- Begin at: Grinnell Glacier Trailhead near Many Glacier Hotel, or take the boat across Swiftcurrent and Josephine Lakes (shortens hike by ~4 miles)
- Notes: The signature hike of the Many Glacier area, climbing to one of the park's most accessible remaining glaciers. The trail passes along turquoise lakes, through alpine meadows, past waterfalls, and ends at the glacier and its milky blue meltwater lake. Grizzly bears, mountain goats, and bighorn sheep are regularly seen. Snow may linger on the upper trail into late July. The boat shuttle across Swiftcurrent and Josephine Lakes significantly reduces the hiking distance and is recommended. One of the most popular hikes in the park — start early.

#### Iceberg Lake
- Distance: 9.6 miles (15.4 km) round trip
- Elevation gain: 1,275 ft (389 m)
- Difficulty: Moderate to Strenuous
- Time: 5-7 hours
- Begin at: Iceberg Lake/Ptarmigan Trail trailhead near Swiftcurrent Motor Inn
- Notes: A spectacular hike through alpine meadows to a cirque lake where icebergs float in the vivid blue water well into summer. The lake sits in the shadow of Mount Wilbur and receives little sunlight, keeping temperatures near freezing year-round. The trail is a gradual climb through wildflower meadows and open terrain — one of the more moderate long hikes in the park. Bear activity is common in this area — hike in groups and carry bear spray. Often combined with or compared to the Ptarmigan Tunnel hike (which branches off from the same trailhead).

#### Cracker Lake
- Distance: 12.8 miles (20.6 km) round trip
- Elevation gain: 1,400 ft (427 m)
- Difficulty: Strenuous (due to distance)
- Time: 6-8 hours
- Begin at: South end of Many Glacier Hotel parking lot
- Notes: A long but rewarding hike to a turquoise glacier-fed lake surrounded by towering cliffs. Less crowded than Grinnell Glacier and Iceberg Lake. The trail follows Canyon Creek through forest and meadows. The stunning blue-green color of the lake comes from glacial silt. Watch for bears, especially in berry season (August-September).

### Two Medicine

#### Dawson-Pitamakan Loop
- Distance: 17.5 miles (28.2 km) loop
- Elevation gain: 2,900 ft (884 m)
- Difficulty: Very Strenuous
- Time: 8-11 hours
- Begin at: North Shore Trailhead at Two Medicine Lake
- Notes: One of the finest loop hikes in the park, crossing two high passes (Dawson Pass at 7,598 ft and Pitamakan Pass at 7,600 ft) with miles of above-treeline hiking and panoramic views of the remote southern peaks. Bighorn sheep and mountain goats are commonly seen. The trail is long and exposed — start early, bring plenty of water and food, and be prepared for weather changes. Snow may persist on the passes into July. This hike requires a full day for strong hikers.

#### Running Eagle Falls (Trick Falls)
- Distance: 0.6 miles (1 km) round trip
- Elevation gain: Minimal
- Difficulty: Easy
- Time: 20 minutes
- Begin at: Running Eagle Falls trailhead on Two Medicine Road
- Notes: A short, flat walk to an unusual waterfall where water flows both over the top and through a hole in the middle of the rock face (the "trick"). In high water (spring/early summer), the upper falls obscure the lower opening. In late summer, the lower falls are more visible. Named for a Blackfeet warrior woman. Wheelchair accessible.

## Permits & Reservations

### Park Entrance Pass
- $35 per vehicle (7-day pass)
- $30 per motorcycle
- $20 per person on foot or bicycle
- Interagency passes accepted
- For 2026, no vehicle reservation is required to drive Going-to-the-Sun Road. A 3-hour parking limit will be enforced at Logan Pass starting July 1.

### Wilderness Permit
Required for all overnight backcountry trips year-round.
- Advance reservations (70% of sites): Lottery on recreation.gov in mid-March for summer season (Jun 15 - Sep 30).
- Walk-up permits (30% of sites): Available at backcountry permit offices on a first-come, first-served basis.
- Cost: $10 per permit (non-refundable).
- Must be picked up in person by 4:30 pm the day before or day of trip start.
- Food hanging required at designated bear poles/bear boxes.

### Campground Reservations
- Reserve at recreation.gov up to 6 months in advance for Apgar, Fish Creek, Many Glacier, St. Mary, and Two Medicine.
- Other campgrounds are first-come, first-served.

### Granite Park and Sperry Chalets
- Book through graniteparkchalet.com and sperrychalet.com.
- Open July through early September. Sell out months in advance.

## Seasons & Access

### Spring (April–June)
A transitional season. Lower elevations are snow-free by April, but Going-to-
the-Sun Road remains closed over Logan Pass — often until late June or early
July depending on snowpack. Snow removal on the road is a major annual
operation. The west side (Apgar to Lake McDonald Lodge) and east side (St. Mary
entrance) are accessible before the road fully opens. Some campgrounds open in
late May. Wildflowers begin at lower elevations. Wildlife is active as bears
emerge from hibernation. Services are limited until late June. A quiet, less
crowded time to visit the accessible portions of the park.

### Summer (July–August)
Peak season. All facilities, roads, and trails are open once Going-to-the-Sun
Road opens fully (typically late June to early July). July is the busiest month
— Logan Pass parking fills by 7-8 am, and popular trailheads in Many Glacier
are full by mid-morning. Temperatures are pleasant (70-80°F in valleys, 50-60°F
at Logan Pass). Wildflowers peak in alpine meadows in late July. Afternoon
thunderstorms are possible. Huckleberry season (late July-August) brings bears
to lower elevations. Use the free shuttle to avoid parking frustration on
Going-to-the-Sun Road.

### Fall (September–October)
Crowds thin dramatically after Labor Day. Larch trees turn brilliant gold in
late September — one of the park's most beautiful seasonal displays. Wildlife
is active as bears prepare for hibernation. Temperatures cool (50-65°F days,
25-35°F nights). First snow can arrive at higher elevations in September. Many
facilities and lodges close in mid-September. Going-to-the-Sun Road typically
closes over Logan Pass in mid-October. Many Glacier Road may close earlier
depending on snow. Fall is an excellent time for photography and solitude.

### Winter (November–March)
Going-to-the-Sun Road is closed over Logan Pass. The road is plowed from the
west entrance to Lake McDonald Lodge. The east side is accessible to St. Mary
but conditions are harsh. Cross-country skiing and snowshoeing on unplowed
roads are popular. Winter backcountry camping is available with permits. Expect
extreme cold, deep snow, and short days. Avalanche danger is significant in
the backcountry. The park is beautiful but demands serious winter preparation.

## Getting There & Around

### Getting There
- Nearest airports: Glacier Park International (FCA, Kalispell — 30 miles from west entrance), Great Falls International (GTF, 150 miles from east side)
- From Kalispell/Whitefish: 30-45 minutes to the West Glacier entrance
- From Missoula: 3.5 hours to the west entrance
- From Great Falls: 3 hours to the east side (St. Mary or East Glacier)
- Amtrak's Empire Builder stops at West Glacier, East Glacier Park, and Essex (seasonal)
- Two main entrance areas: West Glacier (west) and St. Mary (east). Many Glacier and Two Medicine have separate access roads from the east side.

### Getting Around Inside the Park
Free shuttle: Glacier operates a free shuttle along Going-to-the-Sun Road from
late June through Labor Day. Two routes (west side and east side) connect at
Logan Pass. Shuttles run every 15-30 minutes during peak hours. Note: For 2026,
the shuttle system may have changes — check the park website. The shuttle
cannot accommodate overnight gear.

Express shuttle to Logan Pass: For 2026, reservation-based express shuttles to
Logan Pass are planned. Check nps.gov/glac for details and booking.

Driving tips:
- Going-to-the-Sun Road between Avalanche Creek and Sun Point has a 21-foot vehicle length limit and 8-foot width limit. No large RVs or trailers.
- Logan Pass parking (175 spaces) fills by 7-8 am in summer. The 3-hour parking limit (starting July 1, 2026) means more turnover but still arrive early or use the shuttle.
- Many Glacier Road is a 12-mile dead-end road. The parking lot fills early for popular hikes — arrive by 7 am.
- The North Fork area (Bowman Lake, Kintla Lake) is accessed via long, unpaved roads. High clearance recommended.

## Practical Tips
- No vehicle reservation is required for 2026, but Logan Pass has a 3-hour parking limit starting July 1. Arrive early or use the shuttle.
- Bear spray is essential and strongly recommended for all hiking. Available for purchase and rental at gear shops in West Glacier, Whitefish, and at the Apgar Visitor Center.
- Cell service is extremely limited. Coverage is spotty in West Glacier and St. Mary; virtually none in the park interior, Many Glacier, or Two Medicine. Download offline maps before entering.
- Park entrance fee is $35 per vehicle (7-day pass). Annual and interagency passes accepted.
- No gas inside the park. Fill up in West Glacier, St. Mary, East Glacier, or Babb.
- Huckleberries are everywhere in late summer. Bears love them too — be extra alert when hiking through berry patches and make noise.
- The park's glaciers are rapidly retreating. Of the roughly 150 glaciers present in 1850, only 25 named glaciers remain, and scientists estimate they may disappear within the next few decades. Visiting Grinnell Glacier or other accessible glaciers is a time-sensitive experience.
- Pets are allowed only in campgrounds, parking areas, and along roadways (leashed). Pets are NOT allowed on any trail or in the backcountry.
- Water is safe to drink from park taps and campground faucets. Backcountry water must be filtered or treated.
- Going-to-the-Sun Road is a stunning but demanding drive with narrow sections, no guardrails in places, and sheer drop-offs. Drive cautiously and use pullouts to let faster traffic pass.
- Waterton Lakes National Park in Canada connects to Glacier across the international border. The Chief Mountain border crossing (seasonal, typically mid-May through September) provides access. A passport is required.
