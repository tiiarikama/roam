# Zion National Park

## Overview

Zion National Park sits in southwestern Utah and protects 229 square miles of
dramatic sandstone canyons, towering cliffs, and the winding Virgin River. The
park's signature landscape is Zion Canyon — a deep, narrow gorge flanked by
massive walls of cream, pink, and red Navajo Sandstone that rise up to 2,000
feet above the canyon floor.

The park has three main sections. Zion Canyon is the most visited area, home to
iconic trails like Angels Landing and The Narrows, and accessible primarily by
the free park shuttle from March through November. Kolob Canyons, accessed from
Interstate 15 about 40 miles northwest, offers a quieter experience with finger
canyons and access to Kolob Arch. The Kolob Terrace area, reached via Kolob
Terrace Road from the town of Virgin, provides high-elevation access to the
park's backcountry and the Subway.

The gateway town of Springdale sits just outside the south entrance and offers
lodging, dining, gear rentals, and a free town shuttle connecting to the park.

## Entry - Fees & Reservations

Zion National Park is open 24 hours a day, year-round. An entry reservation is not required to enter Zion National Park. All visitors must have or obtain an entrance pass upon arrival to the park.

- Standard Entrance Pass: A Private Vehicle pass costs $35, and a Motocycle pass is $30. A snowmobile pass is $20 and admits one individual. If you enter the park on a bicycle or on foot, and are at least 16 years old, the entry fee is $20. If you enter the park in a motorized vehicle, your entrance pass covers both you and your passengers. Your passengers do not need a separate pass. Admission to Grand Canyon National Park is for seven days and includes the South Rim.
- Annual Pass: Priced at $70 for unlimited visits to Grand Canyon National Park for US citizens and residents. valid one year from month of purchase; it is non-transferable. It admits the pass holder and any accompanying persons in a single, private, non-commercial vehicle, or the pass holder and accompanying immediate family (spouse, children, parents) when entry is by other means (train, shuttle, bicycle, or foot).
- Nonresident Fees: Each non-US resident aged 16 and older visiting Grand Canyon National Park must pay a $100 nonresident fee (in addition to the standard entrance fee), unless admitted with an Annual or America the Beautiful Pass. This includes nonresidents entering as part of a commercial tour group, commercial use authorization (CUA) group, or concessioner tour group.
- Free Entrance Days in 2026:

  - May 25: Memorial Day
  - June 14: Flag Day/President Trump's birthday
  - July 3–5: Independence Day weekend
  - August 25: 110th Birthday of the National Park Service
  - September 17: Constitution Day

  - November 11: Veterans Day

## Activities

At Zion National Park, there are opportunities for all ages, abilities, and interests. If you are not sure which activities fit you the best, you can stop by a visitor center and talk to a ranger. Park staff will help you make the most out of your trip!

### Hiking

Zion offers trails ranging from flat, paved riverside walks to some of the most iconic and challenging hikes in the national park system. The desert environment can be harsh — carry at least one gallon of water per person per day, wear sturdy footwear (not sandals or flip-flops), and check conditions before
heading out.

Longer hikes are located in the Zion Wilderness. These are not loop hikes. Most day hikers will hike out and back the same way. Hikers do not need to travel the full distance, they can turn around at any point and return the way they came. The group size limit for all Zion Wilderness trails, including the Virgin River Narrows beyond the junction with Orderville Canyon, is 12 people.

Important safety rules:

- Your safety is your responsibility. Rangers respond to numerous preventable rescues each year.
- Stay on designated trails. Shortcutting damages fragile desert ecosystems.
- Check the current conditions page at nps.gov/zion before hiking — trail closures are common due to rockfall, flooding, or maintenance.
- Flash floods are deadly. Avoid all narrow canyons when rain or thunderstorms are in the forecast.
- The Virgin River closes to all hiking when flow exceeds 150 CFS (cubic feet per second). The top-down Narrows route closes at 120 CFS.
- Cell service is very limited in the park. Download permits, maps, and weather info before entering.

### Canyoneering

Zion is one of the premier canyoneering destinations in the world, with dozens
of technical slot canyons requiring ropes, harnesses, and rappelling skills.
Popular routes include the Subway (top-down technical route), Keyhole Canyon,
Pine Creek, and Mystery Canyon. A wilderness permit is required for all
technical canyoneering. About half of permits are available in advance on
recreation.gov; the remainder are available as walk-in permits. Group sizes
are limited and vary by canyon.

### Biking

Bicycles are allowed on all park roads but not on hiking trails. The Pa'rus
Trail (paved, 3.5 miles round trip) is open to bikes and connects the Visitor
Center area to Canyon Junction. During shuttle season, cyclists can ride the
Zion Canyon Scenic Drive — a popular option — but must follow safety rules:
always pull over and stop when a shuttle bus is behind you, and never pass a
moving shuttle. Bike rentals, including e-bikes, are available in Springdale.

### Scenic Drives

Three scenic drives provide access to different sections of the park:

Zion Canyon Scenic Drive runs 6.2 miles from Canyon Junction to the Temple of
Sinawava. During shuttle season (March through November), this road is closed to
private vehicles — take the free shuttle. When shuttles aren't running (roughly
December through early March), you can drive it yourself.

Zion-Mount Carmel Highway (Highway 9) connects the south and east entrances
through the 1.1-mile Zion-Mount Carmel Tunnel, with dramatic views of slickrock
and the park's east side. Beginning June 7, 2026, vehicles exceeding size/weight
limits (over 35 ft 9 in long, 7 ft 10 in wide, 11 ft 4 in tall, or 50,000 lbs)
will no longer be permitted on this highway.

Kolob Canyons Scenic Drive runs 5 miles from the Kolob Canyons Visitor Center
(off I-15, Exit 40) to a viewpoint overlooking the Kolob finger canyons. Open
to private vehicles year-round.

### Rock Climbing

Zion's massive sandstone walls attract climbers from around the world. Popular
routes are on formations like Moonlight Buttress, Touchstone Wall, and
Prodigal Sun. No permits are required for day climbing. Overnight wall
bivouacs require a wilderness permit. The soft Navajo Sandstone is fragile —
fixed anchors should be used where available, and new bolt placement is
discouraged. Check at the Wilderness Desk for current route conditions and
seasonal closures (peregrine falcon nesting closures are common February
through August).

### Wildlife Watching

Mule deer are common throughout the park. Bighorn sheep can be spotted on
canyon walls and near the east entrance. California condors occasionally soar
over the canyon — look for their distinctive 9.5-foot wingspan and numbered
wing tags. The park is also home to ringtail cats, grey foxes, and Western
rattlesnakes. Keep your distance from all wildlife and never feed animals.

### Ranger Programs

Free ranger-led programs run year-round and include geology talks, nature
walks, and evening programs. Schedules are posted at visitor centers and in
the park's online calendar. The Junior Ranger program is available for
children.

## Overnight Stays

### Lodging

Zion Lodge is the only lodging inside the park, located along the Zion Canyon
Scenic Drive (shuttle stop #5). It offers hotel rooms and cabins. Reservations
open 13 months in advance and sell quickly for peak season. Book at
zionlodge.com.

Springdale, just outside the south entrance, has numerous hotels, motels, and
vacation rentals at various price points. The town is walkable and connected
to the park via the free Springdale shuttle.

### Camping

- Watchman Campground: 190 sites (including electric hookup sites). Open year-round. Reserve at recreation.gov up to 6 months in advance. Located near the Visitor Center.
- South Campground: 117 sites, no hookups. Open March through November. Reserve at recreation.gov. Adjacent to the Visitor Center and Pa'rus Trail.
- Lava Point Campground: 6 primitive sites at 7,890 ft elevation on Kolob Terrace Road. Open seasonally (typically June through September), first-come first-served. No water.

Campground sites fill very quickly during peak season. Reserve as early as
possible. If campgrounds are full, private campgrounds and RV parks are
available in Springdale, Virgin, and Hurricane.

### Overnight Hiking in Wilderness

A wilderness permit is required for all overnight camping in the backcountry.
Permits can be reserved in advance on recreation.gov or obtained as walk-in
permits at the Wilderness Desk in the Zion Canyon Visitor Center (subject to
availability). Permit cost is $6 per reservation plus $5 per person per night.
Walk-in permits are available the day before or day of entry.

Bear canisters are not required, but proper food storage is essential. Use the
provided storage containers at designated campsites. Human waste carry-out
(WAG bags) is required in many areas including The Narrows.

## Trails

### Zion Canyon Easy Hikes

#### Pa'rus Trail

- Distance: 3.5 miles (563 km) round trip
- Elevation gain: 151.7 ft (46.3 m)
- Difficulty: Easy
- Time: 0.5-2 hours
- Begin at: Zion Canyon Visitor Center (shuttle stop #1, near South Campground) or Canyon Junction (shuttle stop #3).
- Notes: Pa'rus is Paiute for 'bubbling water'. A flat, paved trail along the Virgin River connecting the Visitor Center area to Canyon Junction. This is the only trail in Zion where dogs on leash and bicycles are allowed. Wheelchair accessible. Great for a morning or evening stroll with views of the Watchman, the Towers of the Virgin, and the Court of the Patriarchs. The trail makes a good location for watching the sunset, stargazing, and astrophotography. Wildlife, such as deer, can often be seen in the meadows near the trail. Multiple river access points. Trailside exhibits. Restrooms and water filling stations are available at the Visitor Center. Open year-round, any time of the day. No reservatios or fees apply. Visitors may also take an optional, unpaved connector trail to the Human History Museum (shuttle stop #2) about halfway through the trail. This short path is not designed for wheelchairs and not approved for bikes or pets.

#### Lower Emerald Pools Trail

- Distance: 1.2 miles (1.9 km) to Lower Pool
- Elevation gain: 131.1 ft (40 m)
- Difficulty: Easy (Lower) to Moderate (Middle/Upper)
- Time: 1-2
- Begin at: Emerald Pools Trailhead, across from the Zion Lodge (shuttle stop #5). From the shuttle stop, follow the sidewalk across the Lodge Lawn to the crosswalk. Cross the street and continue across the bridge. Follow the trail on the right to begin hiking Lower Emerald Pool Trail.
- Notes: Lower Emerald Pool trail is a rough, paved trail that leads through pinyon-juniper woodland to the Lower Emerald Pool and ephemeral waterfalls.The pools are fragile and an essential water source for wildlife. Do not go into the water, swimming is not permitted. This trail connects to the other Emerald Pools trails and the Kayenta Trail. Avoid cliff edges. Restrooms and water filling stations are available at the Zion Lodge. Trail may be icy in winter, but is available year round at any time of the day. No pets allowed on the Lower Emerald Pools Trail.

#### Riverside Walk

- Distance: 2 miles (3.2 km) round trip
- Elevation gain: 154.2 ft (47 m)
- Difficulty: Easy
- Time: 1-2 hours
- Begin at: the end of the Zion Canyon Scenic Drive at the Temple of Sinawava (shuttle stop #9). Follow the sidewalk past the shuttle stop and restrooms to begin your hike.
- Notes: The Riverside Walk is a relatively flat and paved trail in the northern end of Zion Canyon. This easy trail follows the Virgin River as the sheer sandstone walls narrow in around you. The Riverside Walk is mostly flat through the first half-mile, although paving is irregular in some sections and minor drop-offs are present. Look for exhibits along the trail to learn about canyon carving, erosion, and the ecological richness of this area.This trail also leads to The Narrows. If you wish to stay dry, you can turn around at the end of the paved trail. Water and restrooms are available at the Temple of Sinawava. Expect crowds, especially midday in peak season. Open any time of the day, year-round. Icy conditions in winter may result in sections of the trail closing for safety. No reservations or additional fees aside from park entrance fees apply. No pets allowed on the Riverside Walk trail.

#### Weeping Rock

- Distance: 0.4 miles (0.6 km) round trip
- Elevation gain: ~100f (30 m)
- Difficulty: Easy
- Time: 15-45 minutes
- Begin at: Weeping Rock Trailhead (shuttle stop #7)
- Notes: Short but steep! From the parking area, you'll cross the bridge and head to the left onto a paved trail to Weeping Rock. Along the way, look for small trailside exhibits and watch for minor drop-offs. The trail ends at a set of steps leading you to a rock alcove with dripping springs. Pit toilets are available at the parking area. No reservations are required, and the trail is normally open all year round. Seasonal closes might be in place due to rock fall, so make sure to check alerts ahead of time. No pets allowed on the Weeping Rock trail.

#### Grotto Trail

- Distance: 1 mile (1.6 km) round trip
- Elevation gain: 49 ft (~15 km)
- Difficulty: Easy
- Time: 15-45 minutes
- Begin at: behind the shuttle stop at the Zion Lodge (shuttle stop #5) or behind the restrooms at The Grotto (shuttle stop #6).
- Notes: The Grotto Trail is an easy, 1.0 mi (1.6 km) round trip hike that connects the Zion Lodge to The Grotto Picnic Area. This relatively flat and shaded hike is good for a leisure walk in the park, or it can be combined with the Emerald Pools and Kayenta Trails to create a loop. Look for wildlife such as birds, turkey, and deer along this walk. Restrooms are available at both the Lodge and the Grotto. The trail is open year round, any time of the day. No pets allowed on the Grotto Trail.

#### Archeology Trail

- Distance: 0.4 miles (0.6 km) round trip
- Elevation gain: ~50 ft (15 m)
- Difficulty: Easy
- Time: 0.5-1 h
- Begin at: across from the entrance to the Visitor Center parking lot.
- Notes: A good introduction to the park for visitors with limited time. Short, but steep. The Archaeology Trail is a short trail that leads to exhibits of prehistoric buildings. There are some low angle views of surrounding mountains.

### Zion Canyon Moderate Hikes

#### Watchman Trail

- Distance: 2.8 miles (4.6 km) round trip
- Elevation gain: 479 ft (1461 m)
- Difficulty: Moderate
- Time: 1.5-2 hours
- Begin at: Watchman Trailhead from the Zion Canyon Visitor Center, follow the sidewalk through the Visitor Center plaza and past the shuttle stop to the crosswalk. Stay along the river to begin the trail.
- Notes: The Watchman Trail is a moderate out-and-back hike accessed from the Zion Canyon Visitor Center. From the relatively flat beginning section of trail by the river, this unpaved trail climbs to a magnificent viewpoint of the Watchman, Temples and Towers, lower Zion Canyon, and the Town of Springdale. The terrain is sandy, rocky, and uneven, with several moderate drop-offs. Good for early morning or late afternoon when other trails are crowded. The trailhead is accessible without riding the shuttle. Restrooms and water filling stations are available at the Visitor Center.Bikes and pets are not permitted on the Watchman Trail.

#### Middle Emerald Pools Trail

- Distance: 2.2 miles (3.5 km) round trip.
- Elevation gain: 332.8 ft (101 m)
- Difficulty: Moderate
- Time: 1-3 hours
- Begin at: Emerald Pools Trailhead across from the Zion Lodge (shuttle stop #5). From the shuttle stop, follow the sidewalk across the Lodge Lawn to the crosswalk. Cross the street and continue across the bridge. Follow the trail on the left to begin hiking Middle Emerald Pools.
- Notes: Middle Emerald Pools Trail is a moderate hike along an unpaved sandstone ledge above the Lower Emerald Pool Trail. The initial ascent is quite steep, bringing you to expansive views of main Zion Canyon below. Further along the trail, tucked into the red sandstone of the mountain, enjoy the shade and flora of pinyon-juniper woodlands. The trail then descends to emerald-colored pools of water above the small ephemeral waterfalls of Lower Emerald Pool Trail. The pools are fragile and an essential water source for wildlife. Do not go into the water. Swimming is not permitted. Avoid cliff edges. Connects to the other Emerald Pools trails and the Kayenta Trail. Restrooms and water filling stations are available at the Zion Lodge. The trail is open year round any time of the day, no reservations or additional fees required. No pets are allowed on the Middle Emerald Pools Trail.

#### Upper Emerald Pools Trail

- Distance: 0.6 miles (0.8km)
- Elevation gain: 75.8 ft (23 m)
- Difficulty: Moderate
- Time: 1-2 hours
- Begin at: the Emerald Pools Trailhead at Zion Lodge (shuttle stop #5) or from The Grotto (shuttle stop #6). If starting the trail from Zion Lodge, Zfollow the sidewalk past the lodge lawn to the crosswalk. Cross the road then continue over the bridge to begin. From that point, hikers can either take the easier Lower Emerald Pool Trail on the right, or take the moderate Middle Emerald Pools Trail on the left. If starting from the Grotto to get to the Upper Emerald Pools Trail, hike the moderate 2 mi (3.2 km) Kayenta Trail until it merges with the Emerald Pools Trails.
- Notes: Upper Emerald Pools Trail is a moderate trail that can be accessed via the Kayenta Trail, Lower Emerald Pools Trail, or Middle Emerald Pools Trail. Climb up stone steps to a sandy path that leads through pinyon-juniper woodlands to the base of towering canyon walls which boast majestic, seasonal waterfalls. Enjoy views of canyon landmarks such as Lady Mountain and The Great White Throne along the way. The trail ends at the sandy shore of Upper Emerald Pool. The pool is fragile and an essential water source for wildlife. Do not go into the water. Connects to the other Emerald Pools trails and the Kayenta Trail. Restrooms and water filling stations are available at Zion Lodge and The Grotto. The trail is open year round any time of the day, no reservations or additional fees required. No pets are allowed on the Upper Emerald Pools Trail.

#### Canyon Overlook Trail

- Distance: 1 mile (1.6 km) round trip
- Elevation gain: 154 ft (47 m)
- Difficulty: Moderate
- Time: 1-2 hours
- Begin at: Canyon Overlook Trailhead located on the East Side of Zion. Follow State Route 9 through the Zion-Mount Carmel Tunnel to the trailhead. Parking is limited, recommended to park on the right just after exiting the tunnel heading east.
- Notes: Canyon Overlook Trail is a moderate, 1 mi (1.6 km) trail on the East Side of Zion. The path begins with a series of sandstone steps with a metal handrail leading visitors over rocky terrain above a dark slot canyon. Near the end of the trail, the view opens up to an expanse of red and cream-colored slickrock. The end of the trail is marked by a fenced cliff edge facing the main Zion Canyon, with excellent views of the Towers of the Virgin. Keep an eye out for bighorn sheep along the trail. Pit toilets are available in the parking area. Parking is extremely limited, be prepared to try multiple times. The trailhead parking lot is very small (about 20 spaces) and fills early — arrive before 9 am. No shuttle access; you must drive. Some exposed sections with drop-offs. This trail is along Highway 9, not the Zion Canyon Scenic Drive, so it's accessible by car year-round.

#### Kayenta Trail

- Distance: ~2 miles (3 km) round trip
- Elevation gain: 204 ft (62 m)
- Difficulty: Moderate
- Time: 2-3 hours
- Begin at: The Kayenta Trailhead is located at The Grotto (shuttle stop #6). Use the crosswalk to reach the bridge over the Virgin River, then take a left up the sandstone steps to begin the hike.
- Notes: A moderate, scenic connector trail between the Grotto and the Emerald Pools area, running along the canyon wall above the Virgin River, before ascending to the Lower and Upper Emerald Pool Trails. Provides panoramic views of Zion Canyon along the way. Long drop-offs are present along this trail. Sandstone steps and sandy terrain. Great views of Zion Canyon. You can either return on the same trail, or make a loop using one of the Emerald Pools trails. Water and restrooms are available at The Grotto.

#### Sand Beach

- Distance: 3 miles (4.8 km)
- Elevation gain: 206.7 ft(63m)
- Difficulty: Moderate
- Time: 1-4 hours
- Begin at: primarily accessible from the Emerald Pools Trailhead at the Zion Lodge (shuttle stop #5). Follow the sidewalk past the lodge lawn to the crosswalk. Cross the road then continue over the bridge. From that point, take a left turn to begin the Sand Bench Trail. This is also where you can access Middle Emerald Pools Trail. Sand Bench is also accessible from the Court of the Patriarchs (shuttle stop #4). From the up-canyon shuttle stop, cross the road to the wooden steps leading down to a sandy trail. At that point, hikers have the option to head up-canyon towards the Zion Lodge by taking a right turn, or continue back towards the Court of the Patriarchs to ascend the longer loop portion of the trail. Hiking only the loop portion of the trail makes the roundtrip closer to 5 miles (8 km).
- Notes: The Sand Bench Trail travels atop the remains of an ancient rockfall. This trail leads hikers along the Virgin River and then ascends a landslide that dammed the Virgin River 8,000 years ago and created a lake that extended to Angels Landing. This trail is a commercial horse trail from March to October (offered through a concessioner - visit https://canyonrides.com/horseback-riding-zion-national-park/), and it receives little shade in the hot, summer sun. Open all year round. Winter travel is possible, but watch for patches of ice. Water and restrooms are available at The Zion Lodge.

### Zion Canyon Strenuous Hikes

#### Angels Landing (West Rim Trail to Scout Lookout + Angels Landing)

- Distance: 5.4 miles (8.7 km) round trip to Angels Landing summit
- Elevation gain: 1,488 ft (454 m)
- Difficulty: Strenuous
- Time: 4-5 hours
- Permit: Required for the final half-mile section from Scout Lookout to Angels Landing summit. No permit needed to hike to Scout Lookout.
- Begin at: The Grotto (shuttle stop #6)
- Notes: One of the most famous hikes in the national park system. The trail climbs via Walter's Wiggles — 21 tight switchbacks — to Scout Lookout, then continues along a narrow ridge with 1,000-foot drop-offs on both sides, with chains for handholds. The final half mile requires a permit, obtained through a seasonal lottery or day-before lottery on recreation.gov. The trail is exposed with little shade — start early in summer. Do not attempt in wet or icy conditions, during thunderstorms, or in high winds. People have died on this trail from falls. Carry plenty of water. Verify your permit is downloaded or printed before entering the park, as cell service is unreliable.

#### The Narrows — Bottom-Up Day Hike

- Distance: Up to 10 miles (16 km) round trip; turn around at any point
- Elevation gain: 200 ft (61 m) over full distance
- Difficulty: Moderate to Strenuous (depending on distance and river conditions)
- Time: 3-8 hours depending on distance
- Permit: Not required for bottom-up day hiking (up to Big Springs, 5 miles upstream)
- Begin at: Temple of Sinawava (shuttle stop #9), via the Riverside Walk
- Notes: An extraordinary river hike through the narrowest section of Zion Canyon, where towering walls rise 1,000+ feet and the canyon narrows to as little as 20 feet wide. You'll spend roughly 60% of the hike wading in the Virgin River. Specialized footwear is strongly recommended — neoprene socks and sturdy water shoes or canyoneering boots with felt or rubber soles. Gear rental is available in Springdale. A walking stick helps with balance on the rocky, uneven riverbed. Water temperatures rarely exceed 65°F even in summer — hypothermia is a real risk, especially in fall and winter when wetsuits or drysuits are needed. Do not hike The Narrows when flash floods are possible, when the Virgin River exceeds 150 CFS, or when the National Weather Service has issued a flash flood warning. Check flow rates at the Visitor Center or online before hiking. Do not drink the river water due to cyanobacteria concerns. The best conditions are typically early summer (June) after the spring runoff and before monsoon season in late summer.

#### The Narrows — Top-Down Through-Hike

- Distance: 16 miles (26 km) one way
- Elevation gain: Net descent of ~1,500 ft
- Difficulty: Very Strenuous
- Time: 10-14 hours (day hike) or 2 days (overnight)
- Permit: Required — wilderness permit via reservation on recreation.gov or walk-in
- Begin at: Chamberlain's Ranch (outside the park, requires shuttle or car drop)
- Notes: The full top-down Narrows hike starts at Chamberlain's Ranch and follows the Virgin River downstream to the Temple of Sinawava. This is one of the premier canyon hikes in the world. Only 40 permits are issued per day. The route requires transportation to the remote Chamberlain's Ranch trailhead — several outfitters in Springdale offer shuttle service. The road to Chamberlain's Ranch can become impassable when wet. For overnight trips, 12 designated campsites are available in the canyon (only 2 accommodate groups larger than 6). The top-down route closes when flow exceeds 120 CFS. Wetsuits or drysuits needed in fall/winter. Carry WAG bags — human waste carry-out is required.

#### West Rim Trail

- Distance: 14.2 miles (22.9 km) one way (top-down from Lava Point to the Grotto), roughly 28-32 miles round trip.
- Elevation gain: 1187 ft (361.8m)
- Difficulty: Very Strenuous
- Time: 4-12 hours for the day hike or 1-2 days overnight
- Permit: Required for overnight camping
- Begin at: Lava Point Trailhead (via Kolob Terrace Road) for top-down; The Grotto (shuttle stop #6) for bottom-up
- Notes: A long, challenging trail traversing the western rim of Zion Canyon with spectacular views into the canyon below. The top-down route is easier since it's mostly downhill. The trail passes through Horse Pasture Plateau, Potato Hollow, and eventually connects to the Angels Landing trail at Scout Lookout. At Scout Lookout, there is a junction with the West Rim Trail and the route to Angels Landing. Many hikers choose to stop here to enjoy the views of Zion Canyon, or to look for the endangered California condors that call the cliffs home. Those looking to continue hiking to the end of Angels Landing will need a permit from recreation.gov. Lava Point is at 7,890 ft elevation and is only accessible when Kolob Terrace Road is open (typically late May through November). Water sources are unreliable — carry all water needed. This trail can also be done as an overnight backpacking trip with several designated campsites along the route. Water and restrooms are available at The Grotto, and restrooms are available at Scout Lookout. There is no water available at Scout Lookout.

### East Side Hikes

#### East Rim Trail

- Distance: 10 miles (16 km) round trip
- Elevation gain: 2,148 ft (655 m)
- Difficulty: Strenuous
- Time: 5-7 hours
- Begin at: East Rim Trailhead on Highway 9 (east side of park)
- Notes: A less-crowded trail passing through Jolley Gulch, ponderosa forest, and Echo Canyon. The trail connects to the former Observation Point Trail from the top. The full trail offers expansive views of Zion Canyon, the White Cliffs, and the distant Kolob Canyons. Limited parking at the trailhead. No shuttle access. Carry plenty of water — no reliable water sources along the trail.

### Kolob Canyons Hikes

#### Timber Creek Overlook Trail

- Distance: 1 mile (1.6 km) round trip
- Elevation gain: 100 ft (30 m)
- Difficulty: Easy
- Time: 30 minutes
- Begin at: End of Kolob Canyons Scenic Drive
- Notes: A short trail to an overlook with views of the Kolob finger canyons, Pine Valley Mountains, and on clear days, the distant North Rim of the Grand Canyon. Excellent at sunset. Far fewer crowds than Zion Canyon.

#### Taylor Creek Trail

- Distance: 5 miles (8 km) round trip
- Elevation gain: 450 ft (137 m)
- Difficulty: Moderate
- Time: 3-4 hours
- Begin at: Taylor Creek Trailhead on Kolob Canyons Scenic Drive
- Notes: A beautiful trail following the Middle Fork of Taylor Creek through a canyon to the impressive Double Arch Alcove. The trail crosses the creek several times — expect wet feet. You'll pass two historic homestead cabins along the way. A great alternative to the crowded Zion Canyon trails.

#### La Verkin Creek Trail to Kolob Arch

- Distance: 14 miles (22.5 km) round trip
- Elevation gain: 700 ft (213 m)
- Difficulty: Strenuous (due to distance)
- Time: 7-9 hours (day hike) or overnight
- Begin at: Lee Pass on Kolob Canyons Scenic Drive
- Notes: A long but relatively gentle trail following La Verkin Creek to Kolob Arch, one of the largest freestanding natural arches in the world. Can be done as a very long day hike or a more comfortable overnight backpacking trip (wilderness permit required for overnight). Several backcountry campsites along the creek. The trail also connects to the Hop Valley Trail and can be combined for longer loop options.

## Permits & Reservations

### Angels Landing Permit

Required for the half-mile chain section from Scout Lookout to the Angels Landing summit. Hiking to Scout Lookout does not require a permit, but everyone who hikes Angels Landing needs to have a permit. Rangers may ask to see permits at any point along the trail at any time of day or night. If you have a permit, do not forget to print or download your permit confirmation email before you start the hike. You may need to show this and identification to a ranger on the trail.

- Permits are required 24/7, year-round.
- Two lottery systems, both on recreation.gov:
  - Seasonal lottery: Applications open quarterly (Spring: Feb 13-25, Summer: Apr 1-20, Fall: Jul 1-20, Winter: Oct 1-20). Select up to 7 preferred dates/times. Ranked choice allocation. It costs $6 to apply for a seasonal permit. The fee covers an application for up to 6 people (including the person filling out the application). This fee is non-refundable. If you are selected in the seasonal lottery, recreation.gov will send you two emails. One will be your permit reservation confirmation, and the other will be a receipt for the $3 per-person permit fee. (Note: If you cancel your permit at least two days before your hike, we will refund the $3 per person fee). If you are not selected, you will receive an email letting you know the outcome. You can consider applying again the day before your hike, or for a future Seasonal Lottery.
  - Day-before lottery: Apply from 12:01 am to 3:00 pm MT the day before your intended hike. Results emailed that evening. It costs $6 to apply for a permit. The fee covers an application for up to 6 people (including the person filling out the application). This fee is non-refundable. For example: If you want to hike on a Tuesday, you need to apply between 12:01 a.m. and 3 p.m. MT on Monday. We will issue permits at 4 p.m. MT on Monday. When permits are awarded, after 4 p.m. the day you apply: If you are selected in the day-before lottery, recreation.gov will send you two emails. One will be your permit reservation confirmation, and the other will be a receipt for the $3 per-person permit fee. If you are not selected, you will be notified, and can consider applying again for a future lottery.
- Permits are non-transferable. The permit holder must carry photo ID.
- The time printed on your permit is when you need to be at the Grotto. Leave time to get to the Grotto before the start time printed on your permit.
- Hiking Angels Landing without a permit is a federal violation — fine up to $5,000 and/or 6 months jail.

### Wilderness Permit

Wilderness Permits are required for all overnight backpacking trips, overnight climbing bivouacs, all through-hikes of The Virgin River Narrows and its tributaries, all canyons requiring the use of descending gear or ropes, and all trips into Left Fork of North Creek (The Subway).

Depending on the type of activity that you are participating in, and the location of your trip, there are various ways to obtain a reservation prior to your trip date.

- First-come, First-served Permits: Walk-in spaces are available for Wilderness trips not already filled by Advance Reservation or Lotteries. Permits must be obtained at the Zion Canyon Visitor Center Wilderness Desk the day before or the day of your trip. Come prepared with your trip itinerary and vehicle(s) information: Make and model, color, state, and license plate number.
- Obtaining the Wilderness Permit: Plan your trip itinerary to allow sufficient time to obtain your permit the day of or the day before your trip during our Hours of Operation (check them at https://www.nps.gov/zion/planyourvisit/hours.htm). Allow for at least 20 minutes to obtain a permit. Come prepared with all vehicle(s) information: make, model, and license plate(s). All Wilderness permits must be obtained in-person at the Zion Canyon Visitor Center Wilderness Desk. The individual listed as the reservation holder must be the one to obtain the permit, please plan accordingly.
- Wilderness Permit Fees:
  - Day Use / Canyoneering Permit Fees
    - Lottery Application Fee or Advanced Reservation Fee: $6 lottery application / reservation fee (non-refundable)
    - Per Person Fee: $10 per person (refundable)
    - The reservation fee (in lieu of the lottery application fee) and per person fee are also charged for first-come, first-served permits that are issued in person.
  - Overnight Permit Fees:
    - Permit Fee: $20 per permit (non-refundable)
    - Per Person Fee: $7 per person per night (refundable)
    - The same fees are charged for reservations as well as first-come, first-served permits that are issued in person.

## Seasons & Access

### Spring (March–May)

One of the best times to visit. Moderate temperatures (Zion Canyon highs
60-85°F), wildflowers blooming, and waterfalls at their most impressive from
snowmelt. The shuttle system resumes in early March. The Narrows may be closed
through spring due to high river flows from snowmelt — check flow rates before
planning a Narrows hike. Kolob Terrace Road and Lava Point are typically still
closed due to snow. The park is busy, especially during spring break and on
weekends. Weather can be variable — bring layers.

### Summer (June–August)

Peak visitation, extreme heat (canyon floor regularly exceeds 100°F), and
monsoon thunderstorms. Start hikes at dawn to avoid the worst heat. Afternoon
thunderstorms are common July through September, bringing flash flood risk to
all narrow canyons. The Narrows is at its highest flash flood risk during
monsoon season. Despite the heat, summer is when all areas of the park are
accessible — Kolob Terrace Road and Lava Point are open, and The Narrows is
typically hikeable (when not closed for flash flood warnings). Parking fills
by 8-9 am daily. Arrive early or park in Springdale.

### Fall (September–November)

Crowds thin after Labor Day, temperatures moderate (highs 70-90°F in
September, 50-70°F by November), and fall colors appear along the Virgin River
and on cottonwood trees in the canyon. This is an excellent time for The
Narrows — flows are typically low, though water temperatures drop and wetsuits
become necessary by October. The shuttle runs through late November. Kolob
Terrace Road may close in late October or November due to snow. Monsoon risk
tapers off by mid-September.

### Winter (December–March)

The quietest season. When the shuttle isn't running (roughly early December
through early March, except the holiday period), you can drive the Zion Canyon
Scenic Drive yourself — a rare treat. Zion Canyon temperatures are mild by
winter standards (highs 40-55°F, lows in the 20s). Snow is possible on the
canyon floor and common at higher elevations. Icy trail conditions are common,
especially on north-facing trails like Angels Landing and the upper portions
of the West Rim Trail — microspikes recommended. The Narrows is generally
impractical in winter due to cold water, ice, and the inaccessibility of
Chamberlain's Ranch. Kolob Terrace Road and Lava Point are closed. Kolob
Canyons Scenic Drive remains open (weather permitting).

## Getting There & Around

### Getting There

- Nearest airports: Las Vegas Harry Reid International (LAS, ~170 miles / 2.5 hours), Salt Lake City International (SLC, ~310 miles / 4.5 hours), St. George Regional (SGU, ~60 miles / 1 hour)
- From Las Vegas: ~2.5 hours via I-15 North to UT-9 East
- From Salt Lake City: ~4.5 hours via I-15 South to UT-9 East
- From the Grand Canyon South Rim: ~4.5 hours via US-89A and UT-9
- From Bryce Canyon: ~1.5 hours via US-89 South and UT-9 West

### Getting Around Inside the Park

The mandatory shuttle system is the defining feature of visiting Zion Canyon:

Zion Canyon Shuttle: Free, no reservation needed. Runs March through November
(plus late December holiday period). Nine stops between the Visitor Center and
the Temple of Sinawava. Shuttles run every 5-15 minutes depending on time of
day and season. A round trip takes about 90 minutes. Private vehicles are not
allowed on the Zion Canyon Scenic Drive during shuttle season.

Springdale Shuttle: Free town shuttle connecting parking areas in Springdale to
the park's pedestrian entrance. Runs every 10-15 minutes during shuttle season.
Starts at 8 am (one hour after the park shuttle begins).

Tips for the shuttle:

- Arrive before 8 am to avoid long waits.
- Board at Stop 2 (Human History Museum) instead of the Visitor Center to skip lines.
- Late afternoon (after 3 pm) also tends to have shorter waits.
- Give yourself plenty of time to catch the last shuttle — if you miss it, you may have to walk up to 8 miles back.

New for 2026: The Zion Gateway Park & Ride in Virgin (launched March 1, 2026)
offers parking and SunTran/shuttle connections to Springdale for $5 one way.
This is especially useful for visitors with large vehicles that cannot drive
the Zion-Mount Carmel Highway after June 7, 2026.

When the shuttle is not running (roughly December through early March), you can
drive the Zion Canyon Scenic Drive yourself, but trailhead parking is very
limited.

The Zion-Mount Carmel Highway, Kolob Terrace Road, and Kolob Canyons Scenic
Drive are always open to private vehicles (weather permitting), as they are
not served by the shuttle.

## Practical Tips

- Cell service is very limited throughout the park. Download all permits, maps, and weather information before entering. The NPS app has downloadable offline content for Zion.
- There is no gas inside the park. The nearest stations are in Springdale (just outside the south entrance), Hurricane (24 miles west), and Kanab (40 miles east).
- Springdale has restaurants, grocery stores, gear rental shops, and an ATM. Inside the park, the only food service is at the Zion Lodge (shuttle stop #5).
- Gear rental for The Narrows (canyoneering boots, neoprene socks, dry suits, walking sticks) is available from several outfitters in Springdale — Zion Adventures, Zion Outfitter, Zion Guru, and others. Renting proper footwear is strongly recommended for river hikes.
- Dogs are allowed only on the Pa'rus Trail (leashed) and in campgrounds. Dogs are not allowed on any other trail, on the shuttle, or in the backcountry.
- The Zion-Mount Carmel Tunnel has vehicle size restrictions. Beginning June 7, 2026, vehicles exceeding 35 ft 9 in long, 7 ft 10 in wide, 11 ft 4 in tall, or 50,000 lbs will not be permitted. Measure your vehicle before arrival.
- Parking fills by 8-9 am year-round. Plan to park in Springdale and use the shuttle, or arrive very early.
- Water is available at the Visitor Center, campgrounds, Zion Lodge, and some shuttle stops. There are no water sources on most trails. Carry more water than you think you'll need.
- Flash floods can occur with little warning, especially during monsoon season (July-September). Never camp or hike in narrow canyons when storms are in the forecast.
- Rock squirrels bite — do not feed or approach wildlife.
