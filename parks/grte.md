# Grand Teton National Park

## Overview

Grand Teton National Park protects 310,000 acres of the Teton Range and the
valley of Jackson Hole in northwestern Wyoming. The Teton Range is one of the
most dramatic mountain fronts in North America — the peaks rise abruptly over
7,000 feet from the valley floor with no foothills to soften the view, creating
an iconic wall of jagged granite summits topped by the 13,770-foot Grand Teton.

The park features pristine glacial lakes (Jenny Lake, Jackson Lake, Leigh Lake,
Phelps Lake), the winding Snake River, sagebrush flats teeming with wildlife,
and deep glacially carved canyons that penetrate the range. Over 200 miles of
trails lead from valley meadows into alpine terrain.

Grand Teton sits immediately south of Yellowstone National Park, and the two
parks are connected by the John D. Rockefeller Jr. Memorial Parkway. Many
visitors combine both parks in a single trip — the south entrance of
Yellowstone is only 10 miles from Grand Teton's northern boundary. The town of
Jackson (12 miles south of the park) is the primary gateway, with an airport,
lodging, dining, and gear shops.

## Activities

### Hiking

Grand Teton has over 200 miles of trails spanning from flat valley strolls to
strenuous canyon hikes and alpine passes. The park's trail system offers an
unusual combination: relatively short approaches from the valley floor lead
directly into dramatic mountain scenery thanks to the Tetons' abrupt rise.
Many of the best hikes follow glacially carved canyons (Cascade, Paintbrush,
Death, Garnet) deep into the range.

Important safety rules:

- This is grizzly and black bear country. Carry bear spray, hike in groups, make noise, and store food properly. Stay at least 100 yards from bears and wolves, 25 yards from all other wildlife.
- Moose are common and can be aggressive, especially cows with calves in spring. Give them a wide berth.
- Weather changes rapidly in the mountains. Afternoon thunderstorms are common in summer — start hikes early and be off exposed ridges and passes by early afternoon.
- High passes (Paintbrush Divide, Hurricane Pass, Static Peak Divide) can retain snow and require ice axe skills well into late July. Check conditions at visitor centers before attempting pass crossings.
- Altitude ranges from 6,400 feet in the valley to over 13,000 feet on the peaks. Acclimate before attempting strenuous hikes. Drink plenty of water.
- The Jenny Lake boat shuttle significantly shortens several popular hikes. It runs every 10-15 minutes and costs $20 round trip / $12 one way.

### Mountaineering

Grand Teton National Park is a premier mountaineering destination. The Grand
Teton (13,770 ft) is the classic objective — the standard Owen-Spalding route
involves technical rock climbing (Class 5.4) and most climbers hire a guide
service (Exum Mountain Guides and Jackson Hole Mountain Guides are the two
authorized concessioners). Guided two-day climbs of the Grand Teton are the
most popular mountaineering experience in the park. No permits are needed for
day climbing; a free registration is required for all climbs involving an
overnight bivouac.

Other popular climbing objectives include Mount Moran, Mount Owen, Teewinot,
and the Cathedral Traverse. The Teton Range offers everything from
beginner-friendly guided climbs to world-class alpine routes.

### Scenic Drives

Teton Park Road runs along the base of the Teton Range between Moose Junction
and Jackson Lake Junction, providing the most dramatic front-row views of the
peaks. Key stops include Teton Glacier Turnout, Jenny Lake, and Cathedral Group
Viewpoint. The road is closed to vehicles from November through April but is
open to cyclists and pedestrians during the spring closure period.

US-26/89/191 (the "outer highway") runs along the eastern edge of the park
through sagebrush flats. Pull-offs at Schwabacher Landing, Snake River
Overlook (Ansel Adams' famous photo location), and Cunningham Cabin offer
classic Teton views with the Snake River in the foreground.

Signal Mountain Summit Road climbs 5 miles to a panoramic viewpoint overlooking
Jackson Lake, the Teton Range, and the Snake River valley. One of the best
viewpoints in the park.

### Water Activities

The Snake River through the park is popular for scenic float trips (gentle,
no rapids in the park section). Several authorized outfitters run guided float
trips from Deadmans Bar or Schwabacher Landing — excellent for wildlife viewing
(moose, eagles, otters, osprey). Kayaking and canoeing are permitted on all
park lakes. Motorboats are allowed on Jackson Lake, Jenny Lake (10 hp limit),
and Phelps Lake. All boats require a park permit and an Aquatic Invasive
Species inspection.

Jenny Lake Boat Tours offers scenic cruises and the popular shuttle service to
the Cascade Canyon trailhead (Hidden Falls/Inspiration Point).

### Fishing

Fishing is permitted with a valid Wyoming state fishing license (no additional
park permit required). The Snake River is renowned for fine cutthroat trout
fishing. Jackson Lake, Jenny Lake, and Leigh Lake also offer good fishing.
Native cutthroat trout are the primary species — catch-and-release is
encouraged. Lake trout in Jackson Lake may be kept. Check the park's fishing
regulations for specific rules by water body.

### Wildlife Watching

Grand Teton is one of the best wildlife parks in the lower 48. The sagebrush
flats along US-26/89/191 and the Antelope Flats Road area are prime for bison,
pronghorn, and coyotes. Moose are frequently seen along the Moose-Wilson Road,
around Oxbow Bend, and near willow-lined streams. Elk herds are large,
especially during the fall rut. Grizzly and black bears are present — Oxbow
Bend and Willow Flats are good viewing spots. Bald eagles and osprey nest along
the Snake River. The National Elk Refuge, adjacent to the town of Jackson,
hosts thousands of elk in winter and offers sleigh ride tours.

### Winter Activities

Grand Teton is spectacular in winter. Teton Park Road is closed to vehicles but
groomed for cross-country skiing (one of the premier Nordic skiing experiences
in the US). Snowshoeing is popular on valley trails. The park is open for
snowmobiling on Jackson Lake and a designated route connecting to Yellowstone.
Backcountry skiing in the Teton canyons is world-class but requires avalanche
training and equipment. The Jackson Hole Mountain Resort (just outside the park)
offers downhill skiing with in-bounds views of the park.

## Overnight Stays

### Lodging

In-park lodging is managed by Grand Teton Lodge Company:

- Jackson Lake Lodge: Grand mid-century lodge with floor-to-ceiling windows framing the Tetons across Jackson Lake. Rooms and cottages. Open mid-May through early October.
- Jenny Lake Lodge: Intimate, upscale cabin property near Jenny Lake. Includes breakfast and dinner. Open early June through early October.
- Colter Bay Village: Cabins and tent cabins on Jackson Lake's east shore. Most affordable in-park option. Open late May through early October.
- Headwaters Lodge & Cabins at Flagg Ranch: Cabins near the Yellowstone border. Open early June through late September.
- Signal Mountain Lodge: Lakefront cabins and rooms on Jackson Lake. Independently operated. Open mid-May through mid-October.

The town of Jackson (12 miles south) has extensive lodging from luxury resorts
to budget motels. Teton Village (Jackson Hole Mountain Resort) also has
accommodations near the park's southern entrance via Moose-Wilson Road.

Reservations for in-park lodging open approximately 1 year in advance and sell
out quickly for July and August.

### Camping

Grand Teton has six campgrounds:

Reservable campgrounds (reserve at recreation.gov):

- Jenny Lake: 49 tent-only sites in a prime location near Jenny Lake. Very popular — reserve well in advance. Open early May through late September.
- Signal Mountain: 81 sites on Jackson Lake. Open mid-May through mid-October.
- Colter Bay: 335 sites (the largest). Mix of tent and RV sites. Open late May through early October.
- Headwaters (Flagg Ranch): 100+ sites near the Yellowstone border. Open early June through late September.
- Lizard Creek: 60 sites on the northern end of Jackson Lake. Quiet and scenic. Open early June through early September.

First-come, first-served:

- Gros Ventre: 300 sites on the east side of the valley. Open early May through early October. The last campground to fill and often has availability when others are full.

No hookups at any campground except Headwaters (electric only for some sites
and full hookups for RVs). Campground reservations open on a rolling 6-month
window on recreation.gov. Jenny Lake and Signal Mountain fill fastest.

### Overnight Hiking in Wilderness

A backcountry camping permit is required for all overnight stays.

- About 30% of permits available by advance reservation on recreation.gov (lottery in early January for the upcoming May-October season).
- About 70% available as walk-up permits, obtained in person the day before the trip start at the Craig Thomas Discovery and Visitor Center, Colter Bay Visitor Center, or Jenny Lake Ranger Station.
- Cost: $20 per permit + $7 per person per night.
- Maximum 10 consecutive nights per permit.
- Bear canisters are required in most backcountry camping zones. Available for loan at visitor centers.
- The backcountry is organized into zones (mountain camping — camp anywhere within the zone) and designated sites (lakeshore and lower elevation — specific tent pads).
- Popular areas (Cascade Canyon, Paintbrush Canyon, Teton Crest Trail zones) fill quickly for advance reservations. Walk-up permits offer the best flexibility.

Note for 2026: Death Canyon Trailhead will be closed for construction during
the 2026 summer season. All camping zones remain open but may need to be
accessed through alternate routes.

## Trails

### Jenny Lake Area

#### Jenny Lake Loop

- Distance: 7.6 miles (12.2 km) loop
- Elevation gain: 400 ft (120 m)
- Difficulty: Easy to Moderate
- Time: 3-4 hours
- Begin at: Jenny Lake Visitor Center or South Jenny Lake area
- Notes: A scenic loop around the second-largest lake in the park with stunning views of the Cathedral Group (Grand Teton, Teewinot, Mount Owen) reflected in the water. The trail can be shortened by taking the Jenny Lake boat shuttle across the lake. The west shore section passes near Hidden Falls and Inspiration Point and is the most scenic portion. Mostly flat with some rocky sections on the west side. Moose are frequently spotted along the trail.

#### Hidden Falls and Inspiration Point

- Distance: 2 miles (3.2 km) round trip from boat shuttle dock; 5.6 miles (9 km) from Jenny Lake Visitor Center via the loop trail
- Elevation gain: 420 ft (128 m) from boat dock
- Difficulty: Easy to Moderate
- Time: 1.5-2 hours from boat dock; 3-4 hours from visitor center
- Begin at: West shore boat dock (via Jenny Lake boat shuttle) or Jenny Lake Visitor Center
- Notes: The most popular hike in the park. The boat shuttle drops you 0.5 miles from Hidden Falls, a dramatic 200-foot cascade on Cascade Creek. A short, steep climb continues to Inspiration Point, a rocky overlook with sweeping views of Jenny Lake and Jackson Hole. This is the starting point for continuing into Cascade Canyon. Expect crowds, especially midday in summer. Take the first shuttle of the day for a quieter experience.

#### Cascade Canyon

- Distance: 9.1 miles (14.6 km) round trip from boat dock to the canyon fork; longer options available
- Elevation gain: 1,100 ft (335 m) to the fork
- Difficulty: Moderate
- Time: 4-6 hours
- Begin at: West shore boat dock (via Jenny Lake boat shuttle)
- Notes: One of the best canyon hikes in the Tetons. After passing Hidden Falls and Inspiration Point, the trail enters the magnificent Cascade Canyon with towering granite walls on both sides and views of the Cathedral Group. The trail climbs gently through the canyon alongside Cascade Creek. At the fork (4.5 miles from the dock), you can turn around or continue to Lake Solitude (add 2.8 miles one way, 1,300 ft more elevation) or Hurricane Pass (add 4.7 miles, 2,600 ft more). Wildlife sightings (moose, bears, pikas) are common in the canyon. Start early to beat afternoon crowds and thunderstorms.

#### String Lake Loop

- Distance: 3.6 miles (5.8 km) loop
- Elevation gain: 200 ft (60 m)
- Difficulty: Easy
- Time: 1.5-2 hours
- Begin at: String Lake Trailhead
- Notes: A flat, scenic loop around String Lake with views of Mount Moran, Teewinot, and the Cathedral Group. Popular for swimming in the shallow, warmer waters of String Lake (by Teton standards). The trail connects to Leigh Lake (add 1.5 miles one way for a lakeshore walk) and to Paintbrush Canyon. A good option for families and those seeking an easy hike with big mountain views.

### Taggart & Bradley Lakes Area

#### Taggart Lake Loop

- Distance: 3 miles (4.8 km) loop
- Elevation gain: 400 ft (120 m)
- Difficulty: Easy to Moderate
- Time: 1.5-2 hours
- Begin at: Taggart Lake Trailhead on Teton Park Road
- Notes: A popular loop hike to a beautiful glacial lake at the base of Avalanche Canyon. Views of the Grand Teton and surrounding peaks from the lake are excellent. The trail passes through areas burned in a 2003 fire, now regenerating. Can be extended to include Bradley Lake (adds ~2 miles) for a longer loop with a second lake.

#### Bradley Lake

- Distance: 5 miles (8 km) round trip (via Taggart Lake loop extension)
- Elevation gain: 600 ft (183 m)
- Difficulty: Moderate
- Time: 2.5-3.5 hours
- Begin at: Taggart Lake Trailhead
- Notes: A larger, deeper glacial lake than Taggart, set in a more enclosed basin with dramatic mountain walls. Less crowded than Taggart Lake. Can be hiked as an extension of the Taggart Lake loop or as a direct out-and-back. Good for a picnic and solitude.

### Death Canyon / Southern Tetons

#### Phelps Lake Overlook

- Distance: 1.8 miles (2.9 km) round trip
- Elevation gain: 420 ft (128 m)
- Difficulty: Easy to Moderate
- Time: 1 hour
- Begin at: Death Canyon Trailhead (note: trailhead closed for construction in 2026 — check for alternate access via the Moose-Wilson Road)
- Notes: A short hike to a stunning overlook of Phelps Lake, set in a glacial cirque at the base of Death Canyon. The viewpoint offers one of the best lake panoramas in the park. The hike can be extended into Death Canyon itself for a longer day (see below).

#### Death Canyon to Patrol Cabin

- Distance: 8 miles (12.9 km) round trip
- Elevation gain: 1,360 ft (415 m)
- Difficulty: Moderate to Strenuous
- Time: 4-6 hours
- Begin at: Death Canyon Trailhead (closed for 2026 construction — check alternate access)
- Notes: Follows Phelps Lake's shore then climbs into the dramatic Death Canyon alongside the creek. The Patrol Cabin at 4 miles is a logical turnaround for a day hike. The canyon is spectacular — steep granite walls, waterfalls, and wildflowers. Less crowded than Cascade Canyon. Can be extended to Static Peak Divide (11,000 ft) for experienced hikers seeking alpine views.

### Paintbrush Canyon-Cascade Canyon Loop

#### Paintbrush-Cascade Loop

- Distance: 19.2 miles (30.9 km) loop
- Elevation gain: 3,900 ft (1,189 m)
- Difficulty: Very Strenuous
- Time: 10-13 hours (day hike) or 2-3 days (backpacking)
- Begin at: String Lake Trailhead
- Notes: Widely considered the best loop hike in the Tetons and one of the finest day hikes in the national park system. The loop climbs through Paintbrush Canyon to 10,720-foot Paintbrush Divide, descends to Lake Solitude, continues through Cascade Canyon past Hidden Falls, and returns via the Jenny Lake Loop or boat shuttle. Panoramic views of the Grand Teton, surrounding peaks, and deep canyons are continuous for miles. The Paintbrush Divide crossing can retain dangerous snow into late July — an ice axe and self-arrest skills may be necessary. Check conditions at the Jenny Lake Ranger Station before attempting. Counter-clockwise (up Paintbrush first) is the standard direction. Start very early (by 6 am) for a day hike. This loop is also the park's most popular 2-3 day backpacking trip.

### Jackson Lake / Northern Park

#### Colter Bay Lakeshore Trail

- Distance: 2 miles (3.2 km) loop
- Elevation gain: Minimal
- Difficulty: Easy
- Time: 1 hour
- Begin at: Colter Bay Village
- Notes: A flat loop along the shore of Jackson Lake with views of the Teton Range across the water. Family-friendly. The Colter Bay Visitor Center has an excellent collection of Native American art and artifacts.

#### Hermitage Point

- Distance: 9.2 miles (14.8 km) loop
- Elevation gain: 450 ft (137 m)
- Difficulty: Moderate (due to distance)
- Time: 4-5 hours
- Begin at: Colter Bay Village
- Notes: A longer lakeshore hike on a peninsula extending into Jackson Lake. The trail passes through forest, meadows, and along the shoreline with views of the Tetons across the water. Moose, bears, and waterfowl are commonly seen. Less crowded than Jenny Lake area trails. A peaceful alternative for a half-day hike.

## Permits & Reservations

### Park Entrance Pass

- $35 per vehicle (7-day pass) — also valid for Yellowstone
- $30 per motorcycle
- $20 per person on foot or bicycle
- Interagency passes accepted
- No timed-entry reservation required

### Backcountry Camping Permit

Required for all overnight stays in the backcountry.

- ~30% available by advance reservation: Lottery on recreation.gov in early January for May-October season.
- ~70% available as walk-up permits: In person, the day before trip start, at Craig Thomas Discovery and Visitor Center, Colter Bay Visitor Center, or Jenny Lake Ranger Station.
- Cost: $20 per permit + $7 per person per night.
- Bear canisters required in most zones (available for loan at visitor centers).
- Max 10 consecutive nights per permit.

### Campground Reservations

- Reserve at recreation.gov on a rolling 6-month window.
- Jenny Lake, Signal Mountain, Colter Bay, Headwaters, and Lizard Creek accept reservations.
- Gros Ventre is first-come, first-served.

### Climbing Registration

- Free registration required for all overnight climbs (bivouacs).
- Register at the Jenny Lake Ranger Station.
- No registration needed for day climbing.

## Seasons & Access

### Spring (April–June)

A transitional season. Valley trails are snow-free by mid-May, but mountain
trails and high passes retain deep snow into July. Teton Park Road opens to
vehicles in late April or May (exact date varies — check nps.gov/grte).
Wildflowers bloom in the valley in June. Wildlife is very active — bears
emerge from hibernation, moose calves appear, and birds return. The Jenny Lake
boat shuttle typically begins in late May. Services are limited in early spring.
Fewer crowds than summer.

### Summer (July–August)

Peak season. All facilities, trails, and roads are open. High passes
(Paintbrush Divide, Hurricane Pass) are typically snow-free by late July. The
Jenny Lake area is very busy — the parking lot and boat shuttle lines fill by
mid-morning. Temperatures are pleasant (70-85°F in the valley, 50-65°F at
higher elevations). Afternoon thunderstorms are common — start hikes early.
Wildflowers peak in alpine meadows in late July. Reserve lodging and camping
months in advance.

### Fall (September–October)

One of the best times to visit. Crowds thin after Labor Day. Cottonwood and
aspen trees turn golden along the Snake River and in the canyons — peak color
is typically late September to early October. The elk rut begins in September
with bugling audible across the valley. Grizzly bears are active in lower
elevations preparing for hibernation. Temperatures cool (50-65°F days,
20-35°F nights). First snow can arrive at high elevations in September. Teton
Park Road typically closes in early November. Jenny Lake boat shuttle ends in
late September. Most lodges close in early October.

### Winter (December–March)

Teton Park Road is closed to vehicles but groomed for cross-country skiing
from mid-December through mid-March — a premier Nordic skiing experience with
Teton views. The outer highway (US-26/89/191) remains open year-round.
Snowshoeing on valley trails. Backcountry skiing in the canyons is world-class
but requires avalanche training. The National Elk Refuge offers winter sleigh
rides among thousands of elk. Jackson and Teton Village have full winter
services. Moose-Wilson Road is closed in winter.

## Getting There & Around

### Getting There

- Nearest airports: Jackson Hole Airport (JAC, inside the park — the only commercial airport within a national park, 8 miles from Jackson), Salt Lake City (SLC, 275 miles / 4.5 hours), Idaho Falls (IDA, 90 miles / 1.5 hours)
- From Jackson: The park begins immediately north of town. Moose entrance is 12 miles north.
- From Yellowstone: The South Entrance of Yellowstone is 10 miles north of Grand Teton's northern boundary via the Rockefeller Parkway.
- From Salt Lake City: ~4.5 hours via I-15 North and US-26 East through Idaho Falls and Swan Valley.
- From Idaho Falls: ~1.5 hours via US-26 East through Swan Valley.

### Getting Around Inside the Park

There is no shuttle system within Grand Teton (except the Jenny Lake boat
shuttle for hikers). A private vehicle is needed.

Key driving distances:

- Jackson to Moose Junction: 12 miles (20 min)
- Moose Junction to Jenny Lake: 8 miles (15 min)
- Jenny Lake to Jackson Lake Junction: 12 miles (20 min)
- Jackson Lake Junction to Colter Bay: 5 miles (10 min)
- Colter Bay to Yellowstone South Entrance: 18 miles (25 min)

Teton Park Road (inner road along the base of the Tetons) is the most scenic
route and provides access to Jenny Lake, trailheads, and mountain views. It is
closed from November through late April/May. US-26/89/191 (outer highway) is
open year-round.

Moose-Wilson Road connects Teton Village to the Moose entrance via a narrow,
partially unpaved road. Excellent for wildlife (moose, bears). No vehicles
over 23 feet. Closed in winter.

Gas is available at Jackson Lake Lodge, Signal Mountain, and Colter Bay
(seasonal). Jackson has year-round gas and all services.

## Practical Tips

- No timed-entry reservation is needed to enter Grand Teton in 2026. The $35 vehicle pass also covers Yellowstone (valid 7 days for both parks).
- Bear spray is essential for all hiking. Available at park stores and in Jackson. Carry it accessible, not buried in your pack.
- The Jenny Lake boat shuttle ($20 round trip, $12 one way) is the best way to access Hidden Falls, Inspiration Point, and Cascade Canyon. It saves about 2 miles of hiking each way. Lines can be long by 9-10 am in summer — go early.
- Cell service is generally available along the highway corridor and in developed areas. Coverage is limited to nonexistent in the canyons and backcountry.
- Jackson Hole Airport (JAC) is inside the park — the only commercial airport located within a US national park. Flights connect to major hubs including Denver, Salt Lake City, Dallas, Chicago, and others. Prices are high in summer.
- Moose-Wilson Road is one of the best spots to see moose and bears. Drive slowly, keep windows up near wildlife, and use pullouts for viewing. The road is narrow with no shoulder.
- Combine Grand Teton and Yellowstone on the same trip. The parks are adjacent — allow at least 2 days for Grand Teton and 3-5 days for Yellowstone.
- Schwabacher Landing and Oxbow Bend are the iconic photography spots — best at sunrise when the Tetons are reflected in still water.
- Pets are allowed in campgrounds, on roads, and within 50 feet of roads (leashed). Pets are NOT allowed on any hiking trail or in the backcountry.
- Water from taps at campgrounds and lodges is safe to drink. Backcountry water must be filtered or treated.
- The town of Jackson has full services: grocery stores, gear shops, medical clinic, restaurants, and the National Museum of Wildlife Art (well worth a visit).
- The Snake River float trips (2-3 hours, multiple outfitters) are an excellent way to see wildlife and scenery without strenuous hiking. Best in early morning.
