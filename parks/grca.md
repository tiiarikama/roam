# Grand Canyon National Park

## Overview

Grand Canyon National Park lies in northern Arizona and protects 277 miles of
the Colorado River and the immense canyon it has carved — a mile deep, up to
18 miles wide, and exposing nearly two billion years of Earth's geologic
history in its layered rock walls.

The park has two developed rims. The South Rim (elevation ~7,000 ft) is open 24 hours a day all
year-round (including all visitor services), receives about 90% of visitors, and is home to Grand Canyon Village with lodges, campgrounds, restaurants, shuttles, and most trailheads.
The North Rim (elevation ~8,200 ft) is typically open mid-May through
mid-November and offers a quieter, more forested experience with far fewer
services. It takes 4 to 5 hours to drive the 215 miles / 346 km between the park's South Rim Village and the North Rim Village.

The inner canyon below the rim is seen mainly by hikers, backpackers, mule riders or river runners. is a harsh desert environment with extreme summer heat (often exceeding 110 °F at the river), very little shade, and limited water. The NPS strongly advises against attempting to hike from the rim to the river and back in a single day — over 250 people are rescued from the canyon each year.

## Entry - Fees & Reservations

Reservations or timed entry are not required to enter the Grand Canyon National Park. Entrance passes are paid for at the entrance stations, where credit and debit cards, as well as America the Beautiful passes are accepted for entry (cash is not accepted at park entrance stations).

- Standard Entrance Pass: A Private Vehicle pass costs $35, and a Motocycle pass is $30. If you enter the park on a bicycle or on foot, and are at least 16 years old, the entry fee is $20. If you enter the park in a motorized vehicle, your entrance pass covers both you and your passengers. Your passengers do not need a separate pass. Admission to Grand Canyon National Park is for seven days and includes the South Rim.
- Annual Pass: Priced at $70 for unlimited visits to Grand Canyon National Park for US citizens and residents. valid one year from month of purchase; it is non-transferable. It admits the pass holder and any accompanying persons in a single, private, non-commercial vehicle, or the pass holder and accompanying immediate family (spouse, children, parents) when entry is by other means (train, shuttle, bicycle, or foot).
- Nonresident Fees: Each non-US resident aged 16 and older visiting Grand Canyon National Park must pay a $100 nonresident fee (in addition to the standard entrance fee), unless admitted with an Annual or America the Beautiful Pass. This includes nonresidents entering as part of a commercial tour group, commercial use authorization (CUA) group, or concessioner tour group.

  - May 25: Memorial Day
  - June 14: Flag Day/President Trump's birthday
  - July 3–5: Independence Day weekend
  - August 25: 110th Birthday of the National Park Service

## Activities

### Hiking

Grand Canyon hiking is unique: every below-rim trail starts with a descent
and ends with the hardest part — the climb back out. There are no easy trails
into or out of the canyon. Adequate water and food consumption are absolutely essential to the success of any Grand Canyon hike, particularly in the summer. Both day hikers and the overnight backpackers must be equally prepared for the lack of water, extreme heat and cold, and isolation characteristic of the Grand Canyon. The majority of Grand Canyon hikers are here for the first time, and although many are avid hikers, they find that hiking the Grand Canyon is very different from most other hiking experiences. They tend to react to the experience in one of two ways, either they can't wait to get back, or they swear they will never do it again. Day hiking permits are not required, but backcountry overnight permits are required for any camping below the rim.

Important safety rules:

- Do NOT attempt to hike from the rim to the river and back in one day, especially May through September.
- Carry and drink plenty of water — at least 1 liter per hour of hiking in summer.
- Eat salty snacks to replace electrolytes; drinking water alone can cause hyponatremia.
- Start early (before dawn in summer) to avoid the hottest part of the day.
- Know your turnaround time and stick to it — hiking down is optional, hiking out is mandatory.
- Upper trails can be extremely icy in winter and early spring; microspikes are recommended.
- Mules have the right of way on all trails.

### Scenic Drives & Auto Touring

Two scenic drives along the South Rim showcase the canyon from different angles.

Hermit Road runs 7 miles west from Grand Canyon Village to Hermits Rest, passing
nine overlooks including Hopi Point (one of the best sunset spots), The Abyss (a
dramatic vertical drop), and Pima Point (with views of the Colorado River). From
March through November Hermit Road is closed to private vehicles — take the free
Hermits Rest (Red) Route shuttle. In December through February you can drive it yourself.

Desert View Drive runs 25 miles east from Grand Canyon Village to Desert View,
passing Yaki Point, Grandview Point, Moran Point, and ending at the historic
Desert View Watchtower designed by Mary Colter. This road is open to private
vehicles year-round. There is no shuttle service along Desert View Drive
(except to Yaki Point via the Kaibab/Orange Route shuttle).

### Mule Rides

Mule trips are a Grand Canyon tradition dating back over a century. Two types
of rides are available on the South Rim, operated by Grand Canyon National Park
Lodges (Xanterra):

Day ride to Havasupai Gardens: A roughly 3-hour round trip that descends the
upper portion of the Bright Angel Trail. Riders must be at least 9 years old,
at least 4 ft 7 in tall, and weigh no more than 200 lbs.

Overnight ride to Phantom Ranch: A two-day, one-night trip that descends to the
canyon floor on the Bright Angel Trail and returns via the South Kaibab Trail,
with an overnight stay at Phantom Ranch. Riders must be at least 10 years old,
at least 4 ft 7 in tall, and weigh no more than 200 lbs. Reservations are
extremely competitive — book up to 15 months in advance.

### River Rafting

The Colorado River through Grand Canyon is one of the world's premier
whitewater experiences. Commercial trips range from 3-4 day partial-canyon
motorized trips to full 16-25 day oar-powered trips covering all 226 river
miles. There are no one-day river trips through the length of the canyon.
Approximately 16 authorized outfitters run commercial trips. Apply through
individual outfitters; most trips book 12-18 months in advance. Non-commercial
(private) river permits are available through a weighted lottery at recreation.gov.

### Ranger Programs & Tours

The park offers free ranger-led programs year-round on the South Rim and
seasonally on the North Rim, including geology talks, history walks, and
evening programs at the Mather Point Amphitheater. Check the park newspaper
(The Guide) for current schedules.

Grand Canyon Conservancy Field Institute offers single-day educational hiking
tours on both rims for individuals, families, and small groups.

### Wildlife Watching

The South Rim is home to elk, mule deer, California condors, and a variety of
birds. California condors — one of the rarest birds in the world — are
frequently spotted soaring over the canyon. They are most often seen near
Bright Angel Lodge, Lookout Studio, and along the Rim Trail. The canyon also
supports bighorn sheep, ringtail cats, and a unique pink subspecies of
rattlesnake found only in the inner canyon.

Keep your distance from all wildlife. Elk can be aggressive, especially during
the fall rut. Squirrels at popular viewpoints like Cedar Ridge bite — do not
feed them. Rock squirrel bites are the most common animal injury in the park.

## Overnight Stays

### Lodging

Southing Rim lodging is available all year, and books up well in advance, especially during spring break, summer months, and fall weekends. Most lodges in Grand Canyon Village are within walking distance of the canyon rim. The South Rim has six lodges managed by two concessioners, plus Phantom Ranch at the canyon floor:

South Rim lodges (Xanterra — Grand Canyon National Park Lodges):

- El Tovar Hotel: The park's premier historic hotel, directly on the rim. Fine dining, full bell service; open year-round.
- Bright Angel Lodge & Cabins: Historic lodge designed by Mary Colter in 1935, on the rim near the Bright Angel Trailhead. Mix of lodge rooms and cabins; open year-round.
- Kachina Lodge: Contemporary rooms directly on the rim; open year-round.
- Thunderbird Lodge: Contemporary rooms directly on the rim; open year-round.
- Maswik Lodge: Nestled in ponderosa pine forest, a short walk from the rim; open year-round.

South Rim lodge (Delaware North — Visit Grand Canyon):

- Yavapai Lodge: The largest in-park lodge, near Market Plaza. Modern rooms in East and West wings (West is pet-friendly); open year-round.

Inner Canyon:

- Phantom Ranch: The only lodging below the rim, accessible only by mule, on foot, or by river raft. Dormitory beds and cabins available. Reservations are by lottery — enter at grandcanyonlodges.com up to 15 months in advance.

North Rim:

- North Rim Lodge: Historic lodge at Bright Angel Point. Note: Due to the 2025 Dragon Bravo Fire, overnight lodging will NOT be available on the North Rim in 2026. Check nps.gov/grca for updates.

Reservations for South Rim lodges open 13 months in advance and fill quickly,
especially for peak season (March-October). If in-park lodging is full,
Tusayan (7 miles south of the South Entrance), Williams (60 miles), and
Flagstaff (80 miles) all offer alternatives.

### Camping

South Rim campgrounds:

- Mather Campground: located in Grand Canyon Village on the South Rim of Grand Canyon National Park, 327 sites, open year-round. Each includes a campfire ring/cooking grate, picnic table, parking space and room for up to six people, three tents, and two vehicles. There are flush toilets and drinking water throughout the campground. No hookups are available, however there is a free dump station. Most RV spaces are pull-through. Reserve at recreation.gov up to 6 months in advance. Near Market Plaza with showers and laundry nearby.
- Desert View Campground: At the East Entrance, Desert View Campground offers a peaceful setting with 49 campsites. 30 feet (9.1 m) combined total length is the maximum length for RVs or vehicles pulling travel trailers. Campsites are available by reservation only at www.recreation.gov and can be made up to 6 months in advance starting Sat. April 11, through the night of Sun. October 18, 2026. Make reservations online or call (877-444-6777). It is possible to make same day call-in and/or online reservations.
- Trailer Village RV Park: the only in-park RV campground with full hookups (sewage, water, and electrical with 30 amp and 50 amp sites available) Open year-round, Trailer Village features paved pull-through sites which can accommodate vehicles up to 50 feet long. Trailer Village is operated by Delaware North. Reservations can be made up to 13 months in advance. If visiting during the busy season (May through October) making reservations 1 year in advance is recommend. Reserve at visitgrandcanyon.com.

North Rim campgrounds:

- North Rim Campground: ~90 sites, typically open mid-May through mid-October. Reserve at recreation.gov. In 2026, the campground opening date is TBD following the Dragon Bravo Fire, but May is a possibility — check recreation.gov for availability.

### Overnight Hiking in Wilderness

A backcountry permit is required for all overnight camping below the rim or at
Tuweep. Permits are managed through a lottery system on recreation.gov.

The lottery opens about 4 months before the trip month (see the schedule at
nps.gov/grca). An early-access lottery awards priority booking slots; remaining
availability opens to the general public on the 1st of the month.

Permit cost: $10 per permit plus $15 per person per night for below-rim areas
and $4 per person per night for above-rim areas. The permit fee is
non-refundable; nightly fees are refundable until 30 days before the trip.

Popular corridor campsites (Bright Angel, Indian Garden/Havasupai Gardens, and
Cottonwood) fill quickly. Plan and apply early.

### Eating

Restaurant and grocery options inside the park are open throuhout the year, and free shuttle buses provide transporation between the Visitor center, campgrounds, and restaurants.

The market plaza (business center of the village) has a general store, and deli.

Yavapai Dining Hall is located north of the Yavapai Lodge parking lot, offering breakfast and dinner. Yavapai Cafe offers coffee and snacks. Yavapai Lodge Tavern is located at the same building as the dining hall, offering lunch and dinner.

Grand Canyon Village Historic District offers El Tovar Dining room and El Tovar Lounge as dining options at the El Tovar Hotel. El Tovar Dining Room is located on the rim, offering breakfast, lunch and dinner. Reservations for dinner are recommended. The lounge is located insidethe hotel, and offers drinks and light meals. The historic district also provides Arizona Steakhouse, Fred Harvey Burger, Bright Angel Fountain, and Bright Angel, all within the Bright Angel Lodge area.

Hermit's Rest and Desert View both have snack options: Hermit's Rest Snack Bar, and Trading Post and Desert View Market and Deli, respectively.

## Trails

### South Rim Day Hikes

#### Rim Trail

- Distance: Up to 13 miles (21 km) one way, Hermits Rest to South Kaibab Trailhead
- Elevation gain: Mostly flat with gentle undulations
- Difficulty: Easy
- Time: Variable — walk any portion
- Begin at: Any viewpoint along the South Rim
- Notes: The Rim Trail extends from the village area to Hermits Rest. Begin from any viewpoint in the Village or along Hermit Road. The Rim Trail offers excellent walking for quiet views of the inner canyon and for visitors who desire an easy hike. The trail follows the canyon edge and connects most South Rim viewpoints. The section between Mather Point and Bright Angel Lodge (about 3.5 miles) is paved, mostly accessible, and offers some of the most iconic views in the park. West of Bright Angel Lodge toward Hermits Rest, the trail becomes unpaved and less crowded. You can use the free shuttle buses to customize your walk — ride the shuttle one direction and walk the other. No water is available west of Bright Angel Lodge. This trail stays on the rim and does not descend into the canyon, making it suitable for all fitness levels. It is an excellent option for sunrise and sunset walks — Hopi Point and Mohave Point are among the best sunset viewpoints.

#### Shoshone Point

- Distance: 2 miles (3.2 km) round trip
- Elevation gain: Minimal
- Difficulty: Easy
- Time: 1 hour
- Begin at: Unmarked pullout on Desert View Drive, about 3.5 miles east of Yaki Point
- Notes: One of the South Rim's best-kept secrets. A flat, easy trail through ponderosa forest leads to a secluded 270-degree canyon viewpoint with far fewer people than the main overlooks. Excellent for sunrise or sunset. The small trailhead parking area (about 20 spaces) is unmarked and easy to miss. No facilities at the trailhead or viewpoint. The area is sometimes reserved for private events on weekends.

#### South Kaibab Trail — Ooh Aah Point

- Distance: 1.8 miles (2.9 km) round trip
- Elevation gain: 600 ft (183 m)
- Difficulty: Moderate
- Time: 1-2 hours
- Begin at: South Kaibab Trailhead (shuttle access only via Kaibab/Orange Route)
- Notes: The shortest and most rewarding way to experience the below-rim canyon. After a couple of switchbacks the trail opens onto exposed ridgeline with sweeping views. Ooh Aah Point is an informal name for a rocky promontory about 0.9 miles down. The trail is steep, exposed, and has no shade or water at any point. There is no parking at the trailhead — you must take the Kaibab Rim (Orange) Route shuttle from the Visitor Center or the Hikers Express shuttle from Bright Angel Lodge (early morning departures). Restrooms and a seasonal water refill station are at the trailhead. This is a good turnaround point for visitors who want to get below the rim without committing to a longer hike.

#### South Kaibab Trail — Cedar Ridge

- Distance: 3 miles (4.8 km) round trip
- Elevation gain: 1,120 ft (340 m)
- Difficulty: Moderate to Strenuous
- Time: 2-4 hours
- Begin at: South Kaibab Trailhead (shuttle access only)
- Notes: Continuing past Ooh Aah Point, the trail descends steeply to Cedar Ridge, a broad flat area with pit toilets and expansive views. This is the NPS-recommended turnaround for most day hikers on the South Kaibab. The mule hitching post and toilet make it a comfortable rest stop. Beware of aggressive squirrels that will go after unattended food. There is no water on the South Kaibab Trail. The climb back up is strenuous — allow roughly twice the time it took to descend. Not recommended to go past this point on a day hike during the summer season.

#### South Kaibab Trail — Skeleton Point

- Distance: 6 miles (9.7 km) round trip
- Elevation gain: 2,040 ft (620 m)
- Difficulty: Strenuous
- Time: 4-6 hours
- Begin at: South Kaibab Trailhead (shuttle access only)
- Notes: Skeleton Point offers the first view of the Colorado River from the South Kaibab Trail. This is a serious day hike — the return climb of over 2,000 feet on an exposed trail with no shade or water is demanding, especially in warm weather. Do not continue past Skeleton Point unless you are an experienced canyon hiker with adequate supplies. The NPS does not recommend hiking beyond Skeleton Point and back in a single day.

#### Bright Angel Trail — 1.5-Mile Resthouse

- Distance: 3 miles (4.8 km) round trip
- Elevation gain: 1,131 ft (345 m)
- Difficulty: Moderate to Strenuous
- Time: 2-4 hours
- Begin at: Bright Angel Trailhead, just west of Bright Angel Lodge in Grand Canyon Village
- Notes: The most popular below-rim trail on the South Rim. The Bright Angel Trail is wider and more gradual than the South Kaibab, with some shade from canyon walls. The 1.5-Mile Resthouse has a shelter and seasonal drinking water (typically May through September — check at the Backcountry Information Center for water status). Restrooms are available. The trailhead is accessible on foot from Grand Canyon Village or via the Village (Blue) Route shuttle. This is a good introductory below-rim hike, though the climb back is steep and tiring. In summer, the NPS recommends turning around here if you start after 10 am.

#### Bright Angel Trail — 3-Mile Resthouse

- Distance: 6 miles (9.7 km) round trip
- Elevation gain: 2,112 ft (644 m)
- Difficulty: Strenuous
- Time: 4-6 hours
- Begin at: Bright Angel Trailhead
- Notes: Continuing past the 1.5-Mile Resthouse, the trail descends through a series of switchbacks called Jacob's Ladder. The 3-Mile Resthouse has a shelter and seasonal drinking water. This is a challenging day hike with a demanding return climb. In summer, start before dawn and carry at least 3 liters of water. The NPS recommends this as the maximum day-hike destination for most visitors during hot months.

#### Bright Angel Trail — Havasupai Gardens (Indian Garden)

- Distance: 9 miles (14.5 km) round trip
- Elevation gain: 3,060 ft (930 m)
- Difficulty: Very Strenuous
- Time: 6-9 hours
- Begin at: Bright Angel Trailhead
- Notes: Havasupai Gardens (formerly known as Indian Garden) is a lush oasis at 3,800 ft below the rim with cottonwood shade, year-round drinking water, restrooms, and a ranger station. This is a very long, strenuous day hike — only for fit, experienced hikers. The return climb is grueling, especially in heat. Do not attempt in summer unless you start well before dawn. An optional 1.5-mile spur trail from Havasupai Gardens leads to Plateau Point, offering views straight down to the Colorado River (adds 3 miles round trip and about 2 hours). Water is available year-round.

#### Hermit Trail — Santa Maria Spring

- Distance: 5 miles (8 km) round trip
- Elevation gain: 1,600 ft (490 m)
- Difficulty: Strenuous
- Time: 4-6 hours
- Begin at: 500 feet west of Hermits Rest (take the Hermits Rest/Red shuttle, or drive in winter)
- Notes: A less-crowded alternative to the corridor trails. The Hermit Trail is unmaintained, steep, rocky, and narrower — hiking boots with good traction are essential. Santa Maria Spring has a small rest shelter; water is available but must be treated before drinking. No mules use this trail. There is no water at the trailhead. For experienced desert hikers.

#### Hermit Trail — Dripping Springs

- Distance: 7 miles (11.3 km) round trip
- Elevation gain: 1,400 ft (430 m)
- Difficulty: Strenuous
- Time: 5-7 hours
- Begin at: 500 feet west of Hermits Rest
- Notes: Branches off the Hermit Trail about 1.5 miles down at the Dripping Springs/Boucher Trail junction. The trail traverses along a ledge with spectacular views before reaching a spring dripping from an overhang. Water from springs must be treated before drinking. Unmaintained trail conditions. For experienced desert hikers.

#### Grandview Trail — Horseshoe Mesa

- Distance: 6.4 miles (10.3 km) round trip
- Elevation gain: 2,600 ft (790 m)
- Difficulty: Very Strenuous
- Time: 5-8 hours
- Begin at: Grandview Point on Desert View Drive, 12 miles east of Grand Canyon Village
- Notes: One of the steepest trails in the park. The Grandview Trail was built in 1893 for copper mining and is unmaintained. The trail descends rapidly through loose rock and steep switchbacks. Horseshoe Mesa offers views into the inner canyon and remnants of the historic Last Chance copper mine. No water is available anywhere on this trail — carry at least 3 liters. No public transportation to the trailhead; drive and park at Grandview Point. For experienced, physically fit hikers only. A shorter option is to hike to Coconino Saddle (2.2 miles round trip, 2-4 hours), which is recommended for a summer day hike.

### North Rim Day Hikes

#### Bright Angel Point Trail

- Distance: 0.5 miles (0.8 km) round trip
- Elevation gain: Minimal
- Difficulty: Easy
- Time: 30 minutes
- Begin at: Log shelter in the parking area by the North Rim Visitor Center, or behind the lodge
- Notes: A short, paved walk to a spectacular overlook with views into Bright Angel Canyon and toward the South Rim. This is the signature North Rim viewpoint and should not be missed. The trail narrows along a ridgeline with drop-offs on both sides. A self-guiding nature trail pamphlet is available at the trailhead.

#### Transept Trail

- Distance: 3 miles (4.8 km) round trip
- Elevation gain: Minimal
- Difficulty: Easy
- Time: 1.5 hours
- Begin at: Grand Canyon Lodge or North Rim Campground
- Notes: A pleasant rim-side walk through forest connecting the lodge and the campground. Canyon views along the way. Good for an early morning or evening stroll.

#### Bridle Trail

- Distance: 2.4 miles (4km) round trip
- Elevation gain: minimal/flat
- Difficulty: Easy
- Time: 1-1.5 hours
- Begin at: Grand Canyon Lodge
- Notes: This trail follows the road as it connects the Grand Canyon Lodge with the North Kaibab Trailhead. Pets on leash and bicycles are permitted on this hard-packed trail.

#### North Kaibab Trail — Coconino Overlook

- Distance: 1.5 miles (2.4 km) round trip
- Elevation gain: 800 ft (245 m)
- Difficulty: Moderate
- Time: 1-2 hours
- Begin at: North Kaibab Trailhead (1.2 miles from the lodge via the Bridle Path)
- Notes: Distance and hiking times vary. This is the only maintained trail into the canyon from the North Rim. Even a short hike to Coconino Overlook (1.5 miles / 2.4 km round-trip) or Supai Tunnel (4 miles / 6.5 km round-trip) can give you an appreciation for the canyon's rich natural beauty and immense size. A hike to Roaring Springs and back is extremely strenuous and takes a full day (7-8 hours) - begin your hike before 7 a.m. Roaring Springs lies 3,050 feet / 930 m below the canyon rim and is 9.4 miles / 15 km round-trip. A day hike beyond Roaring Springs is not recommended. Many years of experience have shown that hikers who proceed beyond this point during the hottest parts of the day have a much greater probability of suffering from heat-related illness, injury, or death. This trail is also used by mules. Round trip to the Colorado River is 28 miles / 45 km and trail descends almost 6,000 ft. / 1,800 m. Under no circumstances should you attempt to hike from the rim to the river and back in one day! Do not hike during the hottest part of the day.

#### North Kaibab Trail — Supai Tunnel

- Distance: 4 miles (6.4 km) round trip
- Elevation gain: 1,450 ft (440 m)
- Difficulty: Strenuous
- Time: 3-4 hours
- Begin at: North Kaibab Trailhead
- Notes: Supai Tunnel has seasonal water and restrooms. A good turnaround point for a half-day hike. The trail is also used by mules — yield the right of way.

#### North Kaibab Trail — Roaring Springs

- Distance: 9.4 miles (15.1 km) round trip
- Elevation gain: 3,050 ft (930 m)
- Difficulty: Very Strenuous
- Time: 7-8 hours
- Begin at: North Kaibab Trailhead
- Notes: An extremely strenuous full-day hike. Start before 7 am. Roaring Springs is the water source for both rims of the park. The NPS does not recommend hiking beyond Roaring Springs as a day hike.

#### Cape Royal Trail

- Distance: 0.6 miles (1 km) round trip
- Elevation gain: Minimal
- Difficulty: Easy
- Time: 30 minutes
- Begin at: Cape Royal parking area at the end of Cape Royal Road
- Notes: A flat, paved trail with canyon views, Angels Window (a natural arch), and views of the Colorado River. Interpretive signs explain the area's natural history. One of the best short walks on the North Rim.

#### Uncle Jim Trail

- Distance: 5 miles (8 km) round trip
- Elevation gain: ~250 ft (75 m)
- Difficulty: Moderate
- Time: 3 hours
- Begin at: North Kaibab Trail parking area
- Notes: Winds through forest to a point overlooking the switchbacks of the North Kaibab Trail. This trail is also used by mules. A good option if you want canyon views without descending below the rim.

## Permits & Reservations

### Backcountry Permit

Required for all overnight camping, other than in developed campgrounds on the North Rim or the South Rim. You must obtain a permit from the Backcountry Information Center. Permits are valid only for the trip leader, itinerary, number of people, and dates specified on the permit. Permits for all overnight backcountry use must be obtained prior to starting a hike.

Last-minute permits are issued by the Backcountry Information Center, located inside the park on the South Rim. The South Rim Backcountry Information Center is open daily, year-round. The office is open on holidays and keep the same daily hours, from 8 am to noon and 1-5 pm Mountain Standard Time. Visitors can also check online at Recreation.gov for cancellations that can be booked up to five days in advance, https://www.recreation.gov/permits/4675337. The last-minute permits are limited to a small number, and are available exclusively for visitors at Grand Canyon National Park who wish to camp at Havasupai Gardens\* or Cottonwood Campgrounds.

You can stop by the Backcountry Information Center at any time during open hours and request a waitlist number. This number is valid for the following morning and will be used to determine priority of service. At 8:00 a.m. Backcountry Information Center staff will call waitlist numbers. When your turn comes you can request a permit, exchange your number for a new waitlist number good for the following day, or simply ask questions. You may participate in the waitlist for as many consecutive days as is convenient.

More permits are available for small groups (1-6 people) than for large groups (7-11 people). Because there are only a few large group sites, limiting the size of your group will increase your chances of obtaining a permit.

Regulations regarding backcountry use are enforced by park rangers, so violations, like backpacking without a permit may result in fines and/or court appearances. Review all regulations listed on your permit and feel free to ask a ranger for clarification, if needed, before beginning your trip. Each individual hiker on your trip is as accountable as the trip leader for abiding by rules and regulations.

- Backcountry permit required for:
  - overnight camping outside of Mather Campground, Desert View Campground and North Rim Campground.
  - overnight camping in all sites at Tuweep Campground
    overnight camping anywhere on the North Rim of Grand Canyon National Park between Oct 16 - May 14 (includes ski-camping)
  - off-river camping by river trip participants
  - overnight camping with private stock outside of Mather Campground
  - overnight camping with private stock on the North Rim
  - packrafting, other than that which occurs under a river permit
- Backcountry permit NOT requireed for:
  - day hikes (this includes nighttime "day" hikes)
  - day stock rides
  - overnight camping at Mather Campground and Desert View Campground (campground reservations at www.recreation.gov)
  - overnight camping at North Rim Campground between May 15 - Oct 15 (campground reservations at www.recreation.gov)
  - overnight camping with private stock at Mather Campground (campground reservations at www.recreation.gov March 1 - Nov 30, private stock allowed in sites HA and HB only)
  - overnight stays at the dormitories or cabins at Phantom Ranch (advanced reservations with Grand Canyon National Park Lodges required)
- Apply through the early-access lottery at recreation.gov/permits/4675337. Find thr "Early Access Lottery" section and select "Register for Open Lottery". You will login to your recreation.gov account. You will need to check a box that says you understand the regulations. Next you click on "Proceed to Cart" to enter your credit card information and pay.
- Lottery applications can be submitted through Recreation.gov over a 2-week period that begins on the 16th of the month and ends at 5pm MST on the first of the month, 4-months prior to the desired start month. Through this lottery, up to 750 applicants will be awarded a date and time when they will be eligible to log in to their Recreation.gov account, check availability, and have an opportunity to reserve an itinerary and pay for their reservation. On the 1st of the subsequent month (e.g., Feb 1 for May start dates) everyone will have immediate access to check availability and reserve any remaining camp space.
- The deadline for early access lottery applications matches the application deadlines.
- Cost: $10 per permit plus $15/person/night below rim or $4/person/night above rim.
- Corridor campsites (Bright Angel Campground, Havasupai Gardens, Cottonwood) fill fastest.
- After the lottery, remaining availability opens to the general public on the 1st of the following month.
- Walk-up permits may be available at the Backcountry Information Center, but are unreliable for popular areas.

### Phantom Ranch Lottery

- Phantom Ranch is the only inner-canyon lodging — dormitory beds and cabins.
- Reservations are by lottery through Grand Canyon National Park Lodges (Xanterra) at grandcanyonlodges.com.
- Lottery entries open up to 15 months in advance. Check general availability by calling 888-297-2757.
- Submissions for Hiker beds and overnight Mule Rides are accepted from 12:01 a.m. on the 1st of the month through 11:59 p.m. on the 25th of the month.

## Seasons & Access

### Spring (March–May)

The best season for below-rim hiking. Temperatures are moderate (South Rim
highs 50-70 °F; inner canyon 70-90 °F) and trails are less crowded than
summer. Wildflowers bloom along the rim and in the inner canyon. The South Rim
is fully operational year-round. The North Rim typically opens May 15 (all
services). Spring break (mid-March through mid-April) brings heavy crowds and
traffic to the South Rim — arrive early or use the shuttle system. Snow and ice
can linger on upper trail sections into April; microspikes may still be useful.
Weather can be variable with occasional late-season snowstorms on the rim.

### Summer (June–August)

Peak visitation with extremely long entrance waits, full parking lots, and
sold-out lodging. Inner canyon temperatures regularly exceed 110 °F. The NPS
strongly discourages below-rim hiking between 10 am and 4 pm. If you do hike,
start before dawn, carry at least 1 liter of water per hour, and eat salty
snacks. Monsoon thunderstorms arrive in July and August, bringing afternoon
lightning, flash floods, and brief heavy rain. Never shelter under trees
during lightning storms on the rim. South Rim highs are 80-85 °F; inner canyon
highs 105-115 °F. The North Rim is fully open and offers cooler temperatures
(70-80 °F).

### Fall (September–November)

Crowds thin significantly after Labor Day. Temperatures moderate to ideal
hiking conditions (South Rim 50-70 °F; inner canyon 70-90 °F). Monsoon storms
taper off by mid-September. The North Rim closes to vehicles in mid-November
(exact date varies — typically around Nov 14-15). Fall colors are visible in
the inner canyon along Bright Angel Creek and at Havasupai Gardens, and in the
North Rim forests. October is widely considered one of the best months to visit.

### Winter (December–February)

The quietest season on the South Rim, which remains open year-round. Expect
snow on the rim (average highs 40-45 °F, lows in the teens). Inner canyon
temperatures are pleasant for hiking (50-60 °F at the river). Upper trail
sections can be extremely icy — microspikes or crampons are essential. The
North Rim is closed to vehicles; the road from Jacob Lake (Highway 67) is not
plowed. Winter visitors to the South Rim enjoy smaller crowds, discounted
lodging rates, and dramatic snow-dusted canyon views. Hermit Road is open to
private vehicles December through February. Chains may be required on park
roads.

## Getting There & Around

### Getting There

- Nearest major airports: Phoenix Sky Harbor (PHX, ~230 miles / 3.5 hours to South Rim), Las Vegas (LAS, ~275 miles / 4.5 hours), Flagstaff Pulliam (FLG, ~80 miles / 1.5 hours)
- From Phoenix: ~3.5 hours via I-17 North to Flagstaff, then US-180 West to AZ-64 North
- From Las Vegas: ~4.5 hours via US-93 South to I-40 East to Williams, then AZ-64 North
- From Flagstaff: ~1.5 hours via US-180 West to AZ-64 North
- The Grand Canyon Railway runs daily from Williams to the South Rim (2.25 hours each way)

### Getting Around Inside the Park

The South Rim has a free shuttle bus system with four main routes:

- Village (Blue) Route: Year-round. Connects the Visitor Center, all lodges, campgrounds, Backcountry Information Center, and Market Plaza. Runs every 10-30 minutes.
- Kaibab Rim (Orange) Route: Year-round. Runs between the Visitor Center, South Kaibab Trailhead, Yaki Point, and Pipe Creek Vista. This is the only way to reach the South Kaibab Trailhead (no private vehicle access).
- Hermits Rest (Red) Route: March through November. Travels 7 miles along the rim with 9 scenic overlooks. Hermit Road is closed to private vehicles during this period.
- Hikers Express: Early morning shuttle from Bright Angel Lodge to South Kaibab Trailhead, with stops at the Backcountry Information Center and Visitor Center. Departs at varying times depending on season (as early as 4 am in summer, 8 am in winter).
- Tusayan (Purple) Route: Seasonal (summer). Free shuttle between the gateway town of Tusayan and the Visitor Center.

The North Rim does not have a public shuttle system. A short early-morning shuttle runs from the lodge to the North Kaibab Trailhead (when the lodge is open).

Trans-Canyon Shuttle provides rim-to-rim transport between the South and North Rims (about 4.5 hours, one-way) daily from mid-May through mid-October. Reserve at trans-canyonshuttle.com.

Parking on the South Rim is extremely limited, especially from March through October. Park at one of the large lots (Lot A near the Visitor Center, or Lot D near the Backcountry Information Center) and use the shuttle.

Desert View Drive is open to private vehicles year-round. There is no shuttle service east of Yaki Point.

Bicycles are welcome on all park roads. The Greenway Trail (paved, ~13 miles) runs along portions of the South Rim and is open to bikes, pedestrians, and wheelchairs.

## Practical Tips

- Cell service is available in Grand Canyon Village on the South Rim (AT&T and Verizon have limited coverage). No service on the North Rim or in the inner canyon. Download offline maps before arriving.
- Gas is available at Desert View (seasonal) on the east end of the park. The nearest year-round gas stations are in Tusayan (7 miles south of the South Entrance) and Cameron (32 miles east via Desert View). There is no gas in Grand Canyon Village.
- Market Plaza in Grand Canyon Village has a general store, post office, bank with ATM, and laundry/showers. A clinic is located in Grand Canyon Village.
- The South Rim sits at ~7,000 ft elevation. Walking at this altitude can be strenuous for visitors not accustomed to it, especially those with heart or respiratory conditions. Stay hydrated and take breaks.
- Water refill stations are located throughout the South Rim developed area. Below the rim, water availability is seasonal and subject to pipeline breaks — always check at the Backcountry Information Center before descending. Carry several gallins of water in your car, especially during summer months.
- Bear canisters are NOT required at the Grand Canyon (unlike many other western parks). However, proper food storage is still important — use provided storage containers at backcountry campsites to deter ringtails and mice.
- Pets are allowed on the Rim Trail, in campgrounds, on greenway trails, and in developed areas. Pets are NOT allowed below the rim, on any inner-canyon trail, or on shuttle buses. Kennel services are available on the South Rim.
- Do not throw anything — including coins, rocks, or food — into the canyon. This is illegal and dangerous to hikers and wildlife below.
- Lightning is a serious hazard during summer monsoon season (July-September). Move away from the rim, exposed ridges, and isolated trees during thunderstorms.
- Bring an extra set of car keys. The nearest locksmith and major auto repair are in Flagstaff (80 miles).
