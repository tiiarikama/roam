# Olympic National Park

## Overview
Olympic National Park protects nearly one million acres of the Olympic Peninsula
in northwestern Washington, encompassing three distinctly different ecosystems:
glacier-capped alpine mountains, lush temperate rainforests, and over 70 miles
of wild Pacific coastline. This extraordinary diversity — all within a single
park — earned Olympic designation as both a UNESCO World Heritage Site and an
International Biosphere Reserve.

The park is organized into several separate regions connected by roads that loop
around (but do not cross through) the Olympic Mountains:
- Hurricane Ridge (north) — alpine meadows and mountain panoramas accessible from Port Angeles
- Hoh Rain Forest (west) — one of the largest temperate rainforests in the US, receiving over 12 feet of rain annually
- Pacific Coast (west) — wild beaches with sea stacks, tide pools, and driftwood-strewn shores
- Sol Duc Valley (north) — hot springs, old-growth forest, and waterfalls
- Lake Crescent (north) — a glacier-carved lake with emerald waters
- Quinault Rain Forest (southwest) — another magnificent rainforest with towering old-growth trees
- Staircase (southeast) — river valley forest (note: currently closed through at least October 2026 due to the 2025 Bear Gulch Fire)

There is no road crossing through the center of the park. Driving between
regions requires traveling around the peninsula on US-101, which can mean 1-3
hours between areas. Plan your visit around specific regions rather than trying
to see everything in one day.

## Activities

### Hiking
Olympic has over 600 miles of trails spanning all three ecosystems. Day hikes
range from flat rainforest loops to strenuous mountain climbs. Most rainforest
and coastal trails are accessible year-round, while alpine trails are typically
snow-free from late June through October.

Important safety rules:
- Rain is a constant — the west side receives 12+ feet annually. Bring waterproof layers and expect wet trails even in summer.
- Coastal hiking requires tide awareness. Some headlands can only be crossed at low tide. Check tide charts and carry them with you. People have been trapped and killed by rising tides.
- Above treeline, weather changes rapidly. Bring layers and be prepared for fog, wind, and cold even in summer.
- Bear canisters are required for all overnight wilderness camping. Black bears are common throughout the park.
- Pets are NOT allowed on any park trail (leashed pets are allowed in campgrounds and on roads only).
- Many trails are muddy year-round, especially in the rainforest. Waterproof boots recommended.

### Hot Springs
Sol Duc Hot Springs Resort offers three mineral hot spring pools (98-107°F) and
a freshwater swimming pool, located within the park along the Sol Duc River.
Open seasonally (typically late March through October). Pool access costs $19
for adults, $15 for children (4-12) and seniors (62+) per session. The resort
also has cabins and a campground. The hot springs are a short drive from the
Sol Duc Falls trailhead.

Olympic Hot Springs, a set of natural thermal pools along the Elwha River
valley, is on undeveloped land. The road to the trailhead is partially closed
to vehicles, requiring a longer hike to reach the springs. Check current access
at the Wilderness Information Center.

### Tidepooling & Beach Exploration
The park's 73 miles of wilderness coast offer exceptional tidepooling. Rocky
intertidal zones teem with sea stars, anemones, mussels, crabs, sea urchins,
and colorful algae. The best spots include Rialto Beach, Ruby Beach, Beach 4,
and Mora area beaches. Always check tide charts — low tide exposes the most
life. Stay on rocks and avoid stepping on organisms. Never remove anything from
tide pools.

### Fishing
Fishing is permitted in the park with a valid Washington State fishing license
and a free Olympic National Park catch record card (available at ranger
stations). The park's rivers support steelhead, salmon (seasonal), and trout.
Lake Crescent is home to the rare Beardslee trout. Regulations vary by water
body and season — check the park's fishing regulations before heading out.

### Wildlife Watching
Olympic supports a remarkable diversity of wildlife. Roosevelt elk (the largest
unmanaged herd in the Pacific Northwest) are commonly seen in the Hoh and
Quinault rain forests and Elwha Valley. Black bears are present throughout the
park. Mountain goats inhabit the alpine zones (a controversial non-native
population that the NPS has been managing). Bald eagles, marbled murrelets, and
northern spotted owls inhabit old-growth forests. On the coast, watch for gray
whales (spring migration), harbor seals, sea otters, and bald eagles. The
park's isolation on the Olympic Peninsula has produced several endemic species
found nowhere else.

### Winter Activities
Hurricane Ridge is the park's winter recreation hub, offering snowshoeing,
cross-country skiing, and occasional downhill skiing at the small Hurricane
Ridge Ski and Snowboard Area (volunteer-operated, weekends and holidays only
when conditions allow). The road to Hurricane Ridge is typically open
Friday-Sunday in winter (weather permitting, chains may be required). Ranger-led
snowshoe walks are offered on winter weekends. The rainforest and coastal trails
are accessible year-round and are arguably most atmospheric in the rainy winter
months.

## Overnight Stays

### Lodging
In-park lodging options:
- Kalaloch Lodge: Oceanfront lodge and cabins on the Pacific coast bluffs. Open year-round. Stunning sunset views. Book at thekalaloclodge.com.
- Lake Crescent Lodge: Historic lodge on the shores of Lake Crescent. Open late April through early January. Book at olympicnationalparks.com.
- Sol Duc Hot Springs Resort: Cabins near the hot springs and hiking trails. Open late March through October. Book at olympicnationalparks.com.
- Log Cabin Resort: Cabins and A-frames on the north shore of Lake Crescent. Open seasonally. Book at olympicnationalparks.com.

Gateway towns with additional lodging:
- Port Angeles: The primary gateway town on the north side. Hotels, motels, restaurants, gear shops. Closest town to Hurricane Ridge.
- Forks: Small town on the west side, gateway to the Hoh Rain Forest and Pacific coast beaches.
- Sequim: East of Port Angeles, in the rain shadow with drier weather.
- Hoodsport and Quinault: Small communities near the south and southwest park entrances.

### Camping
Olympic has 14 campgrounds across its various regions. Most are first-come,
first-served; a few accept reservations.

Reservable campgrounds (reserve at recreation.gov):
- Kalaloch: 170 sites on coastal bluffs overlooking the Pacific. Open year-round. Very popular — reserve well in advance.
- Mora: 94 sites near Rialto Beach. Open year-round. Close to the coast.
- Sol Duc Hot Springs Resort Campground: 82 tent sites + 17 RV sites near the hot springs. Open seasonally. Reserve on recreation.gov (tent/RV) or through the resort (cabins).
- Hoh: 78 sites in the Hoh Rain Forest. Open year-round.
- Heart O' the Hills: 105 sites near Hurricane Ridge road. Open year-round.

First-come, first-served campgrounds include Ozette (15 sites), Fairholme (88 sites on Lake Crescent), Graves Creek (30 sites in Quinault), North Fork Quinault (7 sites), Deer Park (14 sites, high elevation, rough road), and others.

No hookups at any park campground. Most campgrounds have potable water and
flush or vault toilets. Campsite fees are typically $15-$28 per night.

### Overnight Hiking in Wilderness
A wilderness permit is required for all overnight stays in the backcountry
year-round.
- Advanced reservations available on recreation.gov starting April 15 at 7 am PDT for the summer season (May 15 - October 15).
- Walk-up permits available at the Wilderness Information Center in Port Angeles or ranger stations.
- Cost: $6 non-refundable reservation fee + $8 per person per night (ages 16+). Free for 15 and under.
- Bear canisters are REQUIRED for all overnight wilderness trips. Available for loan at the Wilderness Information Center and some ranger stations.
- Maximum group size varies by camp area (6-12 people).
- Camping is only allowed in designated camp areas specified on your permit.

## Trails

### Hurricane Ridge / Alpine Area

#### Hurricane Hill Trail
- Distance: 3.2 miles (5.1 km) round trip
- Elevation gain: 700 ft (213 m)
- Difficulty: Moderate
- Time: 1.5-2 hours
- Begin at: Hurricane Hill trailhead, 1.5 miles past the Hurricane Ridge Visitor Center
- Notes: The most popular alpine hike in the park. A paved-then-gravel trail climbs to the summit of Hurricane Hill with sweeping 360-degree views of the Olympic Mountains, Strait of Juan de Fuca, Vancouver Island, and on clear days, Mount Baker. Wildflowers are spectacular in July and August. The trail is exposed — bring layers for wind and be cautious in changing weather. Snow may linger into July. Marmots are commonly seen along the trail.

#### Klahhane Ridge Trail
- Distance: 7 miles (11.3 km) round trip
- Elevation gain: 1,700 ft (520 m)
- Difficulty: Strenuous
- Time: 4-6 hours
- Begin at: Hurricane Ridge Visitor Center (via Switchback Trail)
- Notes: A challenging hike along a high alpine ridge with some of the best views in the park — deep valleys, jagged peaks, and glaciers. The optional scramble to the Mount Angeles summit adds difficulty and exposure. Not recommended in snow or poor visibility. Wildflower meadows in midsummer. Start early as the parking lot at Hurricane Ridge fills quickly in summer.

### Hoh Rain Forest

#### Hall of Mosses Trail
- Distance: 0.8 miles (1.3 km) loop
- Elevation gain: Minimal
- Difficulty: Easy
- Time: 30-45 minutes
- Begin at: Hoh Rain Forest Visitor Center
- Notes: One of the most magical short walks in any national park. A loop through towering old-growth Sitka spruce and western hemlock draped in thick curtains of club moss and licorice fern. The forest floor is carpeted with ferns, oxalis, and fallen nurse logs. Most atmospheric on misty or rainy days when the moss is vivid green. Extremely popular — the small parking lot fills by mid-morning in summer. Arrive early or visit on a weekday.

#### Spruce Nature Trail
- Distance: 1.2 miles (1.9 km) loop
- Elevation gain: Minimal
- Difficulty: Easy
- Time: 30-45 minutes
- Begin at: Hoh Rain Forest Visitor Center
- Notes: A flat loop through the rainforest along the Hoh River. Less crowded than the Hall of Mosses. Offers views of the milky blue-green glacial river and chances to spot Roosevelt elk grazing in the floodplain. Interpretive signs explain the rainforest ecosystem.

#### Hoh River Trail
- Distance: Variable; up to 17.3 miles (27.9 km) one way to Glacier Meadows
- Elevation gain: Minimal for first several miles; significant for full trail
- Difficulty: Easy (first few miles) to Strenuous (full trail)
- Time: 1-2 hours for a short out-and-back; 2-3 days for the full trail to Glacier Meadows
- Begin at: Hoh Rain Forest Visitor Center
- Notes: A flat, well-maintained trail following the Hoh River deep into the rainforest. The first 5 miles to Five Mile Island are gentle and ideal for a half-day walk through old-growth forest. Beyond that, the trail gradually climbs toward the Blue Glacier on Mount Olympus (the park's highest peak at 7,980 ft). The full trail to Glacier Meadows is a backpacking trip requiring a wilderness permit. Even a short walk of 2-3 miles into the forest is rewarding.

### Sol Duc Valley / Lake Crescent

#### Sol Duc Falls Trail
- Distance: 1.6 miles (2.6 km) round trip
- Elevation gain: 200 ft (60 m)
- Difficulty: Easy
- Time: 45 minutes - 1 hour
- Begin at: Sol Duc Falls trailhead at the end of Sol Duc Road
- Notes: A short walk through old-growth forest to a dramatic three-pronged waterfall on the Sol Duc River. A wooden bridge and viewing platform provide excellent views. One of the most accessible and photogenic waterfalls in the park. Can be extended to Lover's Lane loop (6 miles) which follows the river through old-growth forest back to the Sol Duc Hot Springs area.

#### Marymere Falls Trail
- Distance: 1.8 miles (2.9 km) round trip
- Elevation gain: 200 ft (60 m)
- Difficulty: Easy
- Time: 1 hour
- Begin at: Storm King Ranger Station on Lake Crescent (off US-101)
- Notes: An easy walk through old-growth forest to a 90-foot waterfall. The trail crosses a footbridge and climbs a short staircase to the falls viewpoint. A family-friendly hike easily combined with a stop at Lake Crescent. Can be extended to the much more strenuous Mount Storm King trail (see below).

#### Mount Storm King
- Distance: 4 miles (6.4 km) round trip (including Marymere Falls)
- Elevation gain: 2,000 ft (610 m)
- Difficulty: Very Strenuous
- Time: 3-4 hours
- Begin at: Storm King Ranger Station on Lake Crescent
- Notes: Begins on the Marymere Falls trail, then branches off on a relentlessly steep climb through forest. The maintained trail ends at a sign at approximately 2 miles; beyond this point, an unmaintained scramble with ropes leads to viewpoints overlooking Lake Crescent. The upper section involves steep, exposed terrain with fixed ropes. Only for confident, fit hikers comfortable with exposure. Views of the turquoise lake are spectacular.

### Pacific Coast

#### Rialto Beach to Hole-in-the-Wall
- Distance: 3 miles (4.8 km) round trip
- Elevation gain: Minimal
- Difficulty: Easy (walking on cobbles and sand)
- Time: 1.5-2 hours
- Begin at: Rialto Beach parking area (west of Mora Campground)
- Notes: A beach walk along a dramatic Pacific shoreline to Hole-in-the-Wall, a natural sea arch accessible at low tide. Sea stacks, tide pools, driftwood, and crashing surf make this one of the most scenic coastal walks in the park. Tide pools around the arch are rich with marine life at low tide. Check the tide chart before heading out — the arch is only passable at lower tides. Wear closed-toe shoes (the cobble beach is hard on sandals). Rain and wind are common year-round.

#### Ruby Beach
- Distance: 0.5 miles (0.8 km) round trip
- Elevation gain: 50 ft (15 m) descent to beach
- Difficulty: Easy
- Time: 30 minutes - 1 hour (explore at your pace)
- Begin at: Ruby Beach parking area on US-101 (Kalaloch area)
- Notes: A short trail descends through forest to one of the most iconic beaches in the park, with sea stacks, tide pools, and Abbey Island framed by driftwood. Excellent at sunset. One of the most accessible coastal experiences in the park — right off US-101. No tide restrictions for the beach itself, but tide pools are best at low tide.

#### Second Beach
- Distance: 1.4 miles (2.3 km) round trip to beach; explore further along the shore
- Elevation gain: 200 ft (60 m) descent/ascent
- Difficulty: Moderate (steep descent to beach)
- Time: 1-2 hours
- Begin at: Second Beach trailhead in the town of La Push
- Notes: A short trail through forest descends to a wide beach with towering sea stacks and crashing surf. Many consider this the most beautiful beach in Olympic. At low tide, tide pools are accessible around the sea stacks. The return hike involves climbing back up the steep trail. Can be extended along the coast for wilderness beach backpacking (permit required for overnight). Parking is limited — arrive early in summer.

#### Ozette Triangle (Cape Alava - Sand Point Loop)
- Distance: 9.3 miles (15 km) loop
- Elevation gain: Minimal
- Difficulty: Moderate (distance and terrain)
- Time: 4-6 hours
- Begin at: Ozette Ranger Station
- Notes: A loop hike from Ozette Lake through coastal forest on boardwalk trails to two beaches connected by a 3-mile walk along the Pacific shore. Cape Alava is the westernmost point in the contiguous United States. The beach section features sea stacks, petroglyphs, and tide pools. Check tide charts — some sections of the beach walk require lower tides. This is one of the best day hikes in the park for experiencing the wild Pacific coast.

## Permits & Reservations

### Park Entrance Pass
- $30 per vehicle (7-day pass)
- $25 per motorcycle
- $15 per person on foot or bicycle
- Olympic Annual Pass: $55
- Interagency passes accepted
- Entrance fee stations are at Hurricane Ridge Road, Hoh Rain Forest, Sol Duc, and Staircase. Some areas (Quinault, Ozette, some beaches) do not have staffed entrance stations but a pass is still required.

### Wilderness Permit
Required for all overnight stays in the backcountry year-round.
- Advanced reservations on recreation.gov starting April 15 at 7 am PDT for summer season (May 15 - Oct 15).
- Walk-up permits available at the Wilderness Information Center (Port Angeles) and ranger stations.
- Cost: $6 reservation fee (non-refundable) + $8 per person per night (16+). Ages 15 and under free.
- Bear canisters required for all overnight wilderness trips (available for loan).
- Maximum group size 6-12 depending on area.

### Campground Reservations
- Kalaloch, Mora, Sol Duc, and Hoh accept reservations on recreation.gov.
- Other campgrounds are first-come, first-served.

## Seasons & Access

### Spring (April–June)
Rainforest and coastal areas are accessible year-round and are beautiful in
spring — waterfalls are at peak flow from snowmelt and rainfall. Alpine areas
(Hurricane Ridge) are still snow-covered into June; the road is open daily but
high trails remain buried. Wildflowers begin in lower elevations. Rainfall is
frequent on the west side (the Hoh receives most of its 12+ feet of rain
October through May). Temperatures are cool (highs 50-65°F in lowlands). Crowds
are light before Memorial Day.

### Summer (July–September)
The driest and most popular season. July and August are the only reliably dry
months, with daytime highs of 65-75°F in lowlands and 50-60°F at Hurricane
Ridge. Alpine trails are snow-free from late June through October. Wildflowers
peak in the alpine meadows in July. All campgrounds and facilities are open.
Hurricane Ridge parking lot fills by mid-morning on weekends — arrive early.
The Hoh Rain Forest parking lot also fills quickly. Even in summer, the west
side can get rain, so always carry layers. September is often the best month —
warm, drier, and less crowded.

### Fall (October–November)
Rainfall intensifies, especially on the west side. The rainforest becomes
deeply atmospheric with heavy mist and vivid green moss. Salmon spawning runs
in the Hoh and other rivers attract wildlife. Fall colors are subtle compared
to eastern parks but present in bigleaf maples. Most facilities begin closing
in October. Hurricane Ridge road shifts to weekend-only access. Temperatures
cool (highs 45-55°F). Fewer visitors — a wonderful time for solitude.

### Winter (December–March)
The wettest season. The west side receives enormous rainfall (the Hoh can get
20+ inches in a single month). Coastal and rainforest trails remain open and
are hauntingly beautiful. Hurricane Ridge road is open Fridays through Sundays
(weather and road conditions permitting; chains required). Snowshoeing and
cross-country skiing at Hurricane Ridge. Roosevelt elk herds are large and
visible in the valleys. Storm watching on the coast is dramatic. Most lodges
and many campgrounds are closed except Kalaloch Lodge and a few year-round
campgrounds.

## Getting There & Around

### Getting There
- Nearest airports: Seattle-Tacoma International (SEA, 2.5-3 hours to Port Angeles via ferry or driving), Fairchild International (Port Angeles, limited service)
- From Seattle: ~2.5-3 hours via Bainbridge Island ferry + drive to Port Angeles, or ~3 hours driving around via Tacoma and Olympia
- From Portland: ~4.5-5 hours via I-5 North to Olympia, then US-101 North
- No entrance from the south side connects through — US-101 loops around the peninsula

### Getting Around Inside the Park
There is no shuttle system within Olympic National Park. A private vehicle is
essential. The park's regions are separated by long drives around the peninsula:

Approximate driving times from Port Angeles:
- Hurricane Ridge: 30 minutes (17 miles up a winding mountain road)
- Sol Duc Hot Springs: 1 hour
- Lake Crescent: 30 minutes
- Hoh Rain Forest: 1.5-2 hours
- Rialto Beach (La Push): 1.5 hours
- Ruby Beach / Kalaloch: 2-2.5 hours
- Quinault Rain Forest: 2.5-3 hours

Plan your trip by region. Trying to see Hurricane Ridge, the Hoh Rain Forest,
and the coast all in one day is possible but rushed. Most visitors benefit from
2-4 days to experience the park's diversity.

Gas is available in Port Angeles, Forks, Amanda Park, and other small towns on
US-101. There is no gas inside the park.

## Practical Tips
- Rain is Olympic's defining characteristic. The west side (Hoh, Quinault, coast) is among the wettest places in the contiguous US. Bring waterproof jacket, pants, and boots. Even in summer, rain is possible. The east side and Hurricane Ridge area are drier.
- The park is enormous and spread out. There is no road through the center — all travel is around the perimeter on US-101. Budget significant driving time between regions.
- Cell service is limited to nonexistent in most of the park. Port Angeles and Forks have coverage. Download offline maps before entering.
- Pets are NOT allowed on any trail in the park. They are allowed in campgrounds and within 50 feet of roads (leashed). This is strictly enforced.
- Bear canisters are required for all overnight wilderness camping. Available for loan at the Wilderness Information Center in Port Angeles and at some ranger stations.
- Tide charts are essential for coastal hiking. Several headlands and the Hole-in-the-Wall arch are only passable at low tide. Tidal miscalculations are a serious safety issue.
- Roosevelt elk have the right of way. They are large (up to 1,100 lbs), common on roads and trails, especially in the Hoh and Quinault valleys. Do not approach them.
- Banana slugs are iconic. You'll see these bright yellow slugs throughout the rainforest trails. Do not touch them — oils from human skin can harm them.
- The park entrance fee ($30 per vehicle) is separate from any campground or wilderness permit fees.
- Sol Duc Hot Springs is a highlight — plan time for a soak, especially after a rainy day of hiking.
- The Olympic Peninsula has a strong cultural presence of several Native American tribes (Quinault, Quileute, Makah, S'Klallam, and others). Respect tribal lands and cultural sites.
